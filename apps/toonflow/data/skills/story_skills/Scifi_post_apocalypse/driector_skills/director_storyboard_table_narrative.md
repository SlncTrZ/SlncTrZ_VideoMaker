# Storyboard Table Narrative Technique · Sci-Fi Post-Apocalypse · Technique Reference

---

## I. Storyboard Table Positioning

The storyboard table is the director's core tool for converting script into camera language. Table fields are defined by the director according to project needs (shot number, shot scale, camera movement, duration, characters, events, dialogue, lighting, emotion, transitions, etc.). The following provides technique references specific to the sci-fi post-apocalyptic narrative type.

---

## II. Shot Scale Selection

- **Wide shot is the soul shot of the apocalypse** — Wide shots are used far more frequently in sci-fi post-apocalyptic than in other genres. The macro shock of the ruin panorama, endless wasteland, and civilization relics is the foundation of apocalyptic narrative
- **Survival close-up** — Close-ups in the apocalypse focus not on "beauty" but on "truth": cracked lips, dirt under fingernails, blood seeping through bandages, the last sip of water sloshing in the bottle. Close-ups are the magnifying glass for survival pressure
- **Technological relic shot scale** — Abandoned tech equipment (rusted robots, dead screens, collapsed antenna towers) uses medium shots for full view + close-ups for detail — two shots complete the narrative of "once advanced, now abandoned"
- **Extreme close-ups are extremely restrained** — Extreme close-ups in the apocalypse are reserved for "objects with the most narrative power": a photo, an unsent letter, a child's shoe. At most 1 per entire segment
- **Establishing shots must have "shock + information"** — Post-apocalyptic establishing shots must convey both shock value and survival information: the scale of the ruins (shock) + whether there are threats / resources (information). Get it done with 1-2 wide shots

---

## III. Camera Movement Rhythm

- **Handheld as baseline** — Over 50% of shots with slight handheld movement, conveying the instability of survival
- **Slight shake when safe** — During rest / camp segments, handheld shake is minimal, almost imperceptible but adding a "living presence" compared to a tripod
- **Obvious shake when in danger** — When exploring unknown spaces or hearing unidentified sounds, handheld shake increases, reflecting the character's alertness and unease
- **Slow pan = exploration / search** — Extremely slow horizontal movement simulates a character advancing cautiously; the slower the speed, the stronger the suspense
- **Quick push = crisis / discovery** — When a threat appears or an important discovery is made, a sudden fast push-in maximizes impact
- **Slow pull = revealing desolation** — Slow pull from detail to wide shot, the endlessness of the ruins gradually unfolding. The iconic camera movement of apocalyptic narrative

---

## IV. Duration Control

- **Ruin gaze** — Wide shots of ruins get 5-6s of sufficient duration for the audience to "digest" the destruction before them. Ruin wide shots are not transitions but narrative themselves
- **Survival action at normal pace** — Action shots like searching supplies, walking, on guard: 2-4s, maintaining narrative momentum
- **Crisis shots compressed** — Shots of threat appearance / escape / battle: compressed to 1.5-2s, fast pace conveys urgency
- **Human choice gets full duration** — Moral dilemma shots (whether to abandon the wounded, pull the trigger, share food) get 4-5s, letting the audience and character "agonize" together
- **Golden 6-second rule** — Except for ruin wide shots and one-shot explorations, no single shot exceeds 6s
- **Silent wasteland's blankness** — Wasteland walking segments can have 3-4 consecutive shots with no dialogue, no music, pure environmental sound + footsteps. Total silence duration not exceeding 20s, but enough for the audience to feel "the emptiness of the world"

---

## V. Survival Action Design

- **Suspense in search shots** — Storyboard logic for supply-search segments: wide shot scanning space → medium shot approaching target → close-up inspecting item → result (found / empty-handed). Every search carries the suspense: "is there anything?"
- **Chaos of battle / escape** — Post-apocalyptic combat is not about technique versus technique, but chaos, desperation, and anything-goes. Storyboard using handheld tracking + quick cuts + imperfect composition (half the frame obscured) conveys the real chaos of survival
- **Moral weight of resource struggle** — When competing with other survivors for resources, the storyboard should capture not just "action" but also "eyes": the other's fear, the hesitation in one's own hand, the close-up of supplies being taken from the other's grasp. Let the audience feel the moral cost of every confrontation
- **Long take exploration** — Entering a new ruin space can use a continuous one-shot tracking shot (character pushes door → enters → looks around → discovery), lasting 8-10s, continuously feeding new spatial information to sustain tension
- **Don't break up what one shot can cover** — A single tracking long shot (character searches room → finds item → puts it in backpack) is more immersive than breaking it into three fragmented shots

---

## VI. Human Choice Narrative

- **Moral dilemmas use silence** — Segments facing moral choices (save or not, kill or not, share or not) strip away all music, leaving only environmental sound + character breathing. Let the weight of silence press on the audience's heart
- **Choice shot in three beats** — ① Wide shot showing the dilemma (situation) → ② Character expression close-up (struggle) → ③ Result of action (choice). Three beats, no dragging
- **Trust and betrayal** — The moment of betrayal uses "revealing" camera language: slow pull from the truster's perspective, revealing the betrayer's action → cut to the truster's expression close-up. First show the fact, then the reaction
- **Ritual of sacrifice** — Shots of a character's self-sacrifice get full ritual treatment: last eye contact (close-up cross-cutting) → turning toward danger (full shot from behind) → reaction of those left behind (close-up). Don't rush through it — sacrifice deserves to be gazed upon
- **Kindness's glimmer** — When a character chooses kindness in despair (sharing the last of their food, risking danger to save a stranger), use warm lighting changes + faint warm music fading in. But don't over-sentimentalize — restraint is power

---

## VII. Dialogue & Silence

- **Every word is precious** — In the apocalypse, people have no energy for small talk. Dialogue should be minimal and practical: "Safe," "Let's go," "How much further?" An occasional emotional line ("Thank you for staying") carries extra weight because of its rarity
- **Environmental narrative replaces dialogue** — Don't use dialogue to explain "what happened." Let the audience read the answer from the ruins themselves: scratch marks on a wall, a highway clogged with wrecked cars, a wall covered in missing-person notices
- **Memory fragments** — Dialogue about memories from "before the end" should be extremely short, flashing like fragments ("That day was sunny…" followed by silence). Pair with close-up + defocus / flashback treatment
- **One line of dialogue, one shot** — Every line in the apocalypse carries weight — give each its own shot. Especially lines expressing trust, promises, or farewells
- **Monologue with wide shots** — A character's rare inner monologue pairs with wide shots / empty wasteland frames, the voice sounding as if drifting from "somewhere in this empty world"

---

## VIII. Transition Design

- **Hard cuts within same scene** — Hard cuts between shots within the same space keep the narrative tight
- **Ruin montage** — When changing scenes, quickly layer 2-3 empty ruin shots (1-1.5s each), both conveying geographical shift and reinforcing the apocalyptic atmosphere
- **Signal interference transition** — Screen displays electronic signal interference effects (snow / lines / distortion) as transitions,suggesting the lingering remnants of technological civilization
- **Temporal decay** — Use contrasting shots of the same location at different decay levels (intact → rusted → collapsed) to express the passage of time, suitable for transitions between memory and present
- **Flashback to civilization** — Use short 1-2s flashbacks inserting images of "before the end" (busy streets / laughing people), creating a jarring contrast with the present ruins. Flashback footage should be brighter and warmer in quality, forming a color-temperature contrast with the gray present
- **Walking as inter-segment transition** — Wasteland walking segments are the natural paragraph dividers of apocalyptic narrative. Use 2-3 wide shots to convey movement from point A to point B
