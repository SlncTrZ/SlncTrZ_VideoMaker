# Narrative Technique · Sci-Fi Post-Apocalypse · Technique Reference

---

## I. Thematic Conception & Apocalyptic Core

### Sci-Fi Post-Apocalyptic Narrative Points

- **Survival is the narrative's underlying OS** — Every action in the apocalypse (walking, drinking, sleeping, trusting) is redefined by the premise of "staying alive." Every decision in the narrative should carry the weight of survival cost
- **Civilization's remains are narrative** — Traces of civilization left in the ruins (a family photo, a rusted playground, an abandoned school bus) carry innate narrative power — they silently tell "what was once here." Use environmental narrative instead of dialogue to explain
- **Humanity reveals itself under extremity** — The apocalypse is a natural "humanity laboratory": who gets abandoned when resources run out? Who chooses sacrifice at the brink of death? Who wavers when betrayal offers a way out? Extreme environments amplify what is normally hidden
- **Suggested audience takeaway directions** — Desolate awe / surviving against all odds / humanity's moral test / bitter hope. Avoid directions that mismatch the apocalyptic temperament like "lighthearted," "humorous," or "sweet"
- **The weight of hope is proportional to despair** — Hope in the apocalypse cannot be cheap. The darker the world, the more precious a glimmer of light — a green sprout growing from rubble, a hand reached out in desperation, voices heard in the distance while dying. Hope must have despair as its foundation

## II. Narrative Structure & Survival Rhythm

### Sci-Fi Post-Apocalyptic Narrative Points

- **Crisis-driven narrative** — The engine of apocalyptic narrative is not "what someone wants" but "must survive today." Each segment should have a clear survival crisis (resources running out, threat approaching, shelter lost, companion injured). Crisis drives action, action drives the narrative
- **Resources are conflict** — Food, water, medicine, ammunition, safe shelter — resource scarcity is the root of apocalyptic conflict. The narrative should constantly make the audience feel the urgency of "not having enough"
- **Trust tests escalate** — Trust in the apocalypse is scarcer than resources. The trust line follows a progression of "testing → cooperation → trial → betrayal or steadfastness." Each trust test should carry higher stakes than the last
- **Use the wasteland as breathing room between segments** — Between crisis segments, insert low-intensity segments like wasteland walking or ruin exploration as emotional buffer. These segments are not "empty" but use environmental narrative to enrich the world
- **Hope curve** — The emotional trajectory of apocalyptic narrative is not a continuous descent, but a jagged curve of "despair → glimmer → greater despair → stronger glimmer." After each rock bottom, give a glimmer of hope (finding water, meeting a friendly person, discovering a shelter), then overturn it with a greater blow
- **The ending need not be perfect** — The best ending for apocalyptic narrative is not "everything returns to normal," but "keep walking with scars." Survival itself is victory; there is no need to redeem the whole world

## III. Scene-by-Scene Atmosphere Design

### Sci-Fi Post-Apocalyptic Narrative Points

- **Describe atmosphere with decay** — Don't say "desolate," say "rust has spread to the third step, weeds push through cracked floor tiles." Concrete details of decay hit harder than abstract words
- **Typical apocalyptic scene types and design** —

| Scene Type | Emotional Direction | Narrative Technique | Music Suggestion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ruin Exploration | Alert + Curiosity | Slow advance, scanning the space, discovering civilization relics | Low-frequency hum + occasional metal clang |
| Resource Struggle | Urgency + Confrontation | Searching supplies, competing with others, making trade-offs | Rhythmic background noise, crescendo |
| Rest / Camp | Fatigue + Vulnerability | Gathering around fire, simple meal sharing, brief human warmth | Very quiet guitar or harmonica solo |
| Crisis / Threat | Fear + Survival | Being pursued, shelter lost, companion injured | Heartbeat drum + piercing alarm or complete silence |
| Human Choice | Struggle + Heaviness | Whether to abandon the wounded, whether to open the last can | Music drops out, pure silence |
| Glimmer of Hope | Bitter + Warmth | Discovering a green sprout in the ruins, distant human voices, sunrise | From silence to warm single instrument, gradual fade-in |

- **Spatial decay as temporal narrative** — The degree of environmental decay directly narrates "how long since the apocalypse":
  - **Recent apocalypse**: Buildings mostly intact but empty, items scattered as if people just left
  - **Mid-stage apocalypse**: Rust, overgrown weeds, partial collapse, nature beginning to reclaim territory
  - **Far-stage apocalypse**: Ruins, broken walls, vegetation covering everything, civilization traces reduced to outlines
- **Environment as threat** — In the apocalypse, the environment itself is the enemy: toxic air, unstable buildings, contaminated water, extreme weather. The storyboard should always make the audience feel the hostility of the environment
- **Shot intent should write "survival feeling" not "scene description"** — "Using a wide shot to make the audience feel human insignificance and helplessness amidst the endless ruins" is better than "using a wide shot to film the ruins"

## IV. Sound & Music Direction

### Sci-Fi Post-Apocalyptic Narrative Points

- **Silence is the apocalypse's undertone** — The most unsettling thing about the apocalypse is not the roar of monsters, but "a place that should have sound but doesn't": an empty city, a factory that no longer runs, a silent playground. Silence itself narrates "extinction"
- **Industrial waste noise** — Metal friction, pipe leaks, the sizzle of short-circuiting wires, distant unidentified rumbles — these "sounds of civilization's remains" form the unique soundscape of the apocalypse
- **Extreme restraint in scoring** — Overall music coverage should not exceed 30%. The power of the apocalypse lies in the "sense of vacuum"; too much scoring weakens the impact of desolation
- **Narrative function of alarms/signal tones** — Sounds from remaining electronic devices (signal-free broadcasts, looping emergency announcements, intermittent beeping) are the "ghost sounds" unique to the apocalypse, highly atmospheric
- **Music emotion model** —

| Emotional Stage | Music Strategy | Coverage |
|---|---|---|
| Ruin Exploration | Low-frequency hum + occasional metallic sounds | Low |
| Daily Survival | No music, pure environmental sound | None |
| Crisis Approaching | Heartbeat drum, crescendo | Medium |
| Human Choice | Complete silence | None |
| Battle / Escape | Rhythmic electronic background noise + percussion | Medium-High |
| Glimmer of Hope | Single warm instrument, slow fade-in | Low → Medium |

- **One sound sets the atmosphere** — A single sudden sound in the apocalypse has more impact than a rich soundscape: a gunshot in the distance, a door creaking open in the wind, a cry for help from the rubble. In complete silence, one sound is an event

## V. Composition & Shot Scale Narrative

### Sci-Fi Post-Apocalyptic Narrative Points

- **Three core compositions and their narrative functions** —
  - **Ruin wide shot** — A tiny human figure embedded in a vast expanse of ruins, conveying the apocalyptic sense of "civilization is gone, humans are ants." The most frequently used composition in sci-fi post-apocalyptic
  - **Oppressive space** — Moving through low ceilings / narrow passages / collapsed ruins; physical spatial oppression directly conveys the suffocation of survival
  - **Human vs. ruin contrast** — Placing characters alongside civilization relics (a person standing before an abandoned skyscraper / walking on an abandoned highway), using scale contrast to narrate civilization's fall
- **Center composition's "last person" feel** — Center composition in the apocalypse suggests the extreme loneliness of "you're the only one left in these ruins," an iconic shot for a lone wanderer amidst the ruins
- **Dutch angle's sense of disorder** — Dutch angles in the apocalypse convey the psychological suggestion of "the world has tilted / fallen into disorder," suitable for early disaster scenes, mental breakdown, and extremely dangerous environments
- **Foreground ruin obstruction** — Using ruin debris as foreground to partially obscure the subject, creating a sense of "peeking at survivors through the ruins" / searching
- **Shot scale as survival state** —
  - **Extreme wide / wide shot** — Full view of ruins, endless wasteland, macro shock of civilization's demise
  - **Full shot** — Positional relationships of survivors in space, direction of threats
  - **Medium shot** — Survival actions (searching, on guard, walking), character interaction
  - **Close-up** — Survival details (cracked lips, dust-covered hands, the last can of food), emotional focus
- **Extreme close-up as "relic object"** — Extreme close-ups in the apocalypse often focus on "relics of civilization": a faded family photo, a phone screen with no power, a dust-covered doll. These details carry a thousand words of narrative

## VI. Camera Movement & Rhythm

### Sci-Fi Post-Apocalyptic Narrative Points

- **Handheld is the basic language of the apocalypse** — Over 50% of shots use slight handheld camera work, conveying the survival anxiety of "instability / anything could happen at any moment." Shake level grading: slight shake when safe → obvious shake when in danger → violent shake during combat
- **Slow panning exploration** — Ruin exploration segments use extremely slow horizontal movement, simulating a character cautiously advancing. The slower the speed, the more tense the audience ("what will we see next?")
- **Quick push = crisis outbreak** — When a threat suddenly appears, a fast push-in simulates the physiological reaction of "being startled / impacted"
- **Slow pull = revealing desolation** — Slow pull from a character / detail to a wide shot, revealing the endlessness of the surrounding ruins. The most classic "sighing camera movement" in apocalyptic narrative
- **Segments without stabilizers** — Escape / battle segments deliberately avoid stabilizers, using real camera shake to convey chaos and fear
- **Camera movement as survival state** — Static = safety / alert; slow pan = exploration / search; handheld tracking = movement / unease; quick push = crisis / discovery; slow pull = despair / loneliness
- **360° panning of ruins** — When a character arrives at a new location, use a slow 360° pan to let the audience and character "scan" the surroundings together to assess safety. Serves both as establishing shot and suspense
