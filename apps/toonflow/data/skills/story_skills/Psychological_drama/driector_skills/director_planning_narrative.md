# Narrative Technique · Psychological Drama · Technique Reference

---

## I. Theme and Emotional Core

### Psychological Drama Narrative Points

- **Calm surface, turbulent undercurrent** — The core appeal of psychological drama lies in "inconsistency between appearance and reality." Every seemingly ordinary conversation has subtext, every smile could be a carefully calculated strategy. Theme should revolve around "the invisible offensive and defensive battle between people"
- **Information is a weapon** — The underlying engine of psychological drama narrative is "information asymmetry." Who knows what, who doesn't know what, who pretends not to know — the concealment, speculation, and revelation of information constitute the core of narrative tension
- **Multi-layer cognitive game** — The highest level of psychological drama is not simple deception, but a "cognitive nesting doll": A thinks they are tricking B → B actually knew all along → A knows B knows but pretends not to know. The more layers, the stronger the audience's intellectual pleasure
- **Suggested departure feeling** — Mind-bending / chills down the spine / sudden realization / eerie. Avoid purely sensory directions like "bloody" or "violent"
- **The grayness of human nature** — In psychological drama narratives, there are no absolute good or bad people. Every character has their own logic, circumstances, and bottom line. The best game is when "the audience understands every character's motivation but doesn't know whose side to take"

## II. Narrative Structure and Rhythm Planning

### Psychological Drama Narrative Points

- **Information manipulation is basic skill** — The core task of psychological drama narrative is controlling the flow of information. During the narrative planning phase, it must be clear: what does the audience know in each segment, what does Character A know, what does Character B know. The information gap between the three is the source of tension
- **Perspective switching creates cognitive flips** — Presenting the same event from different character perspectives. Each time the perspective switches, the audience's understanding of the event flips. Mark "perspective switch points" and the cognitive update each switch brings in the planning
- **Reversals need foreshadowing** — Every reversal must have enough clues laid earlier. The audience should feel "I should have known!" rather than "This is so sudden." Suggested ratio offoreshadowing to reversal: 3:1 (three parts setup for one reversal)
- **Tension accumulation → brief release → greater tension** — The rhythm of psychological drama spirals upward: a brief relaxation after each game round, but the stakes of the next round are higher and the risks greater
- **"Exposure" rhythm model** — The core rhythm of psychological drama is the gradual exposure of characters' "cards": disguise → crack appears → other side detects → counterattack → deeper disguise → greater exposure. Each round is closer to the truth than the last
- **Audience position design** —Clarify in each segment whether the audience is in "God's perspective" (knowing both sides' cards) or "following perspective" (only knowing one side's information). The two positions create completely different kinds of tension: God's perspective = anxiety of watching the character walk into a trap; Following perspective = shock of being tricked alongside the character

## III. Scene-by-Scene Emotion Design

### Psychological Drama Narrative Points

- **Emotional goals use game-related vocabulary** — Don't say "tense," say "smiling while talking but palms are sweaty." Don't say "scared," say "discovering the other side knows but not knowing how much they know." The emotions in a game are always multi-layered and contradictory
- **Typical emotional segments and design** —

| Segment Type | Emotional Direction | Narrative Technique | Music Suggestion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Surface Peace | Polite + Undercurrent | Normal conversation but every line has subtext | Extremely light background or no music, letting the dialogue tension operate on its own |
| Probing Confrontation | Tension + Intellectual pleasure | Back-and-forth dialogue with hidden offense and defense, words carrying hidden meaning | Low-frequency undercurrent, rhythm subtly accelerating |
| Crack Appears | Unease + Alertness | A detail exposed, the other side's expression changes in a flash | A dissonant interval flashes briefly, then returns to calm |
| Showdown/Reversal | Shock + Sudden realization | Cards revealed, all previous clues suddenly connect | Sudden silence, or a single low note sustained |
| Stalemate Confrontation | Suffocating + Balance | Both sides know the other knows, but neither moves first | Completely silent, only environmental sound and breathing |
| Final Revelation | Overturning + Reflection | The final truth is one layer deeper than everyone thought | Minimalist instrumental or lingering echoes |

- **"Dialogue is the battlefield" is a core narrative tool** — In psychological drama, dialogue is not information delivery, but an offensive and defensive weapon:
  - **Active questioning** = probing/attacking (trying to extract information)
  - **Avoiding/changing the subject** = defending (not wanting to expose information)
  - **Counter-questioning** = counterattack (pushing the pressure back to the other side)
  - **Silence** = the strongest card (making the other side anxious and expose themselves)
- **Space is the power field** — In psychological drama scenes, whoever occupies the active position in space has the upper hand in the game: sitting at the other end of the table = equal game; standing up and walking around = exerting pressure; turning one's back to the other = confidence/disdain; trapped in a corner = disadvantage
- **Micro-expressions are the truth** — The advanced technique of psychological drama narrative is "the mouth lies but the body tells the truth." In narrative planning, every game segment should mark the character's "true reaction" (micro-expressions, unconscious small movements), which are clues for the audience

## IV. Sound and Music Direction

### Psychological Drama Narrative Points

- **Silence is the strongest weapon** — Key confrontation segments in psychological drama should prioritize removing background music, leaving only environmental sound and the characters' breathing/heartbeats. Dialogue in silence is more tense than any music
- **Music creates cognitive suggestion (suggestion)** — The music in psychological drama is not for heightening atmosphere, but for guiding the audience's cognitive judgment. Music accompanying a certain character's entrance suggests "this person has issues"; a calm conversation paired with unsettling low notes suggests "there's a trap"
- **Deceptiveness of sound** — Music can cooperate with the narrative to "trick" the audience: using warm music to make the audience think a certain character is good, then the change in music at the reversal is itself a narrative tool for "ah, so that's it"
- **Environmental sound is psychological space** — Echo in a sealed space = trapped/no escape; conversation in a noisy environment = secretive/not convenient to tell the truth; empty and quiet space = nowhere to hide/everything is heard
- **Music emotional progression model** —

| Emotional Stage | Music Strategy | Coverage |
|---|---|---|
| Surface Peace | Extremely light background or no music | Extremely low |
| Undercurrent Stirring | Low-frequency dissonant notes, volume gradually increasing | Medium-low |
| Confrontation Escalating | Rhythmic suggestion, tension increasing | Medium |
| Showdown/Reversal | Sudden silence or one strong note followed by silence | Extreme |
| Finale | Minimalist instrumental, leaving aftertaste | Low |

- **"Heartbeat exposure"** — In key game segments, use the character's heartbeat as a sound effect. Accelerating heartbeat suggests the character is lying/tense/about to be exposed. But use it sparingly; making it a "lie detector" for the character will reduce the suspense

## V. Composition and Shot Scale Narrative

### Psychological Drama Narrative Points

- **Narrative functions of the three core compositions** —
  - **Symmetrical composition** — Two people sitting/standing opposite, conveying "evenly matched / confrontation / game balance"
  - **Triangular composition** — The third party in a three-person game (observer/variable/judge), breaking the binary opposition and adding complexity
  - **Unbalanced composition** — Subject biased to one side, the other side left empty or containing a threatening element, suggesting "balance broken / someone in the shadows"
- **Shot scale is information power** — Close-up = the audience is allowed to see micro-expressions (mind-reading); Medium shot = the audience can only see body language (guessing); Long shot = the audience can only see position relationships (judging the layout). Use shot scales to control the audience's "mind-reading permission"
- **Offense-defense in dialogue shot scales** — The shot scale of shot-reverse-shot in dialogue should change with the game situation: the side with the upper hand gets near shots (confidence/composure); the disadvantaged side gets close-ups (tension/micro-expression exposure); when evenly matched, use two-person medium shots
- **Spatial depth is the game layout** — Use spatial depth to convey game relationships: foreground = current speaker (surface); midground = listener (observing/calculating); background = third party or hidden information
- **Mind-reading function of extreme close-ups** — Extreme close-ups (pupils/corner of the mouth/fingertips) are used to capture "flaws when the character lies" or "fleeting changes when found out they've been tricked." These are clues for the audience and must be precise
- **"Things that shouldn't be seen"** — Advanced composition technique in game narrative: let the audience "accidentally" see things through the composition that the character is trying to hide (hands trembling behind the back, fists clenched under the table, a fleeting shift in the eyes)

## VI. Camera Movement and Rhythm

### Psychological Drama Narrative Points

- **Absolute stillness in confrontation segments** — In core psychological drama confrontation segments, the camera should be completely still, like "holding one's breath." 60% or more of shots are static camera positions. Any camera movement will distract the audience from micro-expressions and dialogue subtext
- **Slow push = approaching truth/pressure** — Extremely slow push suggests (suggests) "the audience/character is approaching the truth" or "one side is exerting pressure on the other." The slower the speed, the stronger the oppression
- **Slow pull = revealing the full picture/discovery** — Slowly pulling back from a partial view to reveal the full picture the character hasn't noticed (someone listening in the shadows, evidence right behind them), a classic technique for "the audience knows but the character doesn't"
- **Subtle shift** — In a static frame, the camera shifts extremely slowly to one side, suggesting "the scales of the game are tipping." Whoever it shifts toward is losing advantage
- **Fast cuts for reversal moments** — The only time fast cuts are allowed in psychological drama is at the "cards revealed" reversal moment: rapid shot scale jump cuts (full shot → close-up → extreme close-up) simulating the psychological impact of "sudden realization"
- **Camera movement is psychological situation** — Stillness = confrontation/balance; Slow push = approach/pressure; Slow pull = revelation/retreat; Shift = scales tipping; Fast cut = truth exposure
