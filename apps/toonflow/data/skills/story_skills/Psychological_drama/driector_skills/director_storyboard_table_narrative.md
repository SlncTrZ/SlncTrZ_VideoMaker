# Storyboard Table Narrative Techniques · Psychological Drama · Technique Reference

---

## I. Storyboard Table Positioning

The storyboard table is the director's core tool for transforming script into camera language. Form fields are set by the director according to project needs (shot number, shot scale, camera movement, duration, characters, events, dialogue, lighting, emotion, transitions, etc.). The following only provides technique references under the psychological drama narrative type.

---

## II. Shot Scale Selection

- **Close-up is a mind-reading tool** — Close-ups are used more frequently in psychological drama narratives than in other genres. Close-ups are used to capture the micro-expression flaws in characters where "what they say differs from what they think": a fleeting shift in the eyes, an imperceptible twitch at the corner of the mouth, pupil contraction
- **Medium shot is the home turf of gameplay** — The primary shot scale for dialogue game segments is medium/near shot, ensuring the audience simultaneously sees the character's expressions and body language. Pure close-ups lose body language information; pure long shots lose expression information
- **Long shot sets the scope (layout)** — Long shots are used to describe the "layout" of the game: who is with whom, where the third party is, the power position relationships in the space
- **Extreme close-up is clue deployment** — Extreme close-ups (fingertip trembling / pupil change / fleeting mouth twitch) are used to deploy (deliver) key clues to the audience: this person is lying, this person has discovered something, this person is about to act
- **Establishing shot must subtly hide information** — The establishing shot in game narrative should not only describe the space, but convey the attributes of the "game field" in the establishing shot itself: closed/open, whether there is a third party, where the escape route is

---

## III. Camera Movement Rhythm

- **Default still, stillness is confrontation** — 65% or more of shots should be static camera positions. In psychological drama, the stillness of the camera conveys the confrontation sense of "two people testing each other, whoever moves first reveals a flaw"
- **Extremely slow push = approach/pressure** — Slower than a normal slow push,suggesting "the truth is being approached" or "one side is exerting psychological pressure"
- **Slow pull = reveal/discovery** — Slowly pulling back from a character to reveal information the character doesn't know (someone is behind them / evidence is on the table / another character's reaction)
- **Subtle shift = the scales tipping** — In a static frame, the camera shifts extremely slowly to one side,suggesting the game situation is changing
- **Fast cuts only for reversals** — Quick shot scale jump cuts (medium shot → close-up → extreme close-up) are allowed at the moment of reversal revelation,simulating cognitive shock. Fast cuts are prohibited in all other segments

---

## IV. Duration Control

- **Dialogue shots 3-5s** — The shot-reverse-shot rhythm of game dialogue is slightly slower than everyday conversation. The silence between each sentence is the psychological time when "both sides are calculating"
- **Silent confrontation 4-6s** — Wordless staring/silent segments need sufficient duration for the audience to feel "something is happening in the air"
- **Micro-expression close-ups 2-3s** — The duration of micro-expression flaw close-ups must be precise: too short and the audience won't see it; too long and it becomes deliberate emphasis
- **Reversal moment 1-2s** — The key reaction shot when the cards are revealed must be short and precise, fast enough that the audience is stunned before they can react
- **Long shot/layout description 3-4s** — Long shots for describing the game layout don't need to be too long; just enough information is sufficient
- **Golden rule: silence is longer than dialogue** — In psychological drama, the silence between two sentences should be longer than the sentences themselves. Silence is the game between the two sides "playing their cards"

---

## V. Shot Merging Strategy (De-AI-ification)

- **Maintain shot-reverse-shot in dialogue segments** — Unlike other genres, psychological drama dialogue segments require precise shot-reverse-shot to convey the offense-defense rhythm. Don't forcibly merge into two-person shots. Every switch is the "ball" passing between the two people
- **One game round as a group** — A complete "offense-defense round" (probe → response → re-probe → show cards) should be planned as a group of shots, maintaining internal rhythm continuity
- **Extremely high narrative density** — Every shot in psychological drama must carry information (subtext of dialogue / micro-expression / body language clues / spatial suggestion). Pure atmosphere shots are not allowed
- **Director's mindset check** — Self-check after writing the storyboard: does every shot have an "information layer"? What can the audience "read" in this shot? If nothing can be read, this shot shouldn't exist

---

## VI. One-Shot Take (Long Shot Merging)

- **Applicable conditions** — Two characters changing positions in space, power relationships shifting with spatial position, transitioning from one information state to another
- **Typical scenes** — Character A stands up and walks to the window (from equal footing to looking down), characters walking around a table (power display), walking from the meeting room to the corridor (from public to private)
- **Marking method** — Write the complete path in the camera movement field (e.g., "One-shot take: Character A stands up → slow push follows to the window → pan brings in Character B's reaction in the seat → end frame two-person composition"),describe the start and end frames in the image description
- **Duration relaxation** — Game long takes, due to continuously updated information density, can break the single-shot 6s upper limit, but not exceed 12s
- **Spatial displacement is psychological displacement** — In a long take, every spatial movement of a character should correspond to a change in the psychological game situation

---

## VII. Character Interaction Design

- **Dialogue is confrontation** — Every line of game dialogue in the storyboard table should mark "subtext" or "true intention." Dialogue is the surface; subtext is the game
- **Micro-movements are flaws** — In the storyboard table, design a subtle "true reaction" movement for the character in every game shot: unconsciously touching the ear = nervousness; finger lightly tapping the table = calculating; drinking water = stalling for time
- **Whoever speaks first loses** — In game confrontation, silence is a weapon. The storyboard table should indicate "who the silence belongs to" — who creates the silence (actively exerting pressure), who bears the silence (passively defending)

---

## VIII. Dialogue and Silence

- **Every sentence has two meanings** — In psychological drama, there is no "ordinary dialogue." Dialogue in the storyboard table should be concise and ambiguous, letting the audience discover on a second watch that "so that's what that sentence meant"
- **Silence is more dangerous than dialogue** — The most dangerous moment in a game is not an argument, but when one side suddenly stops talking. Silence means "I've already thought of the next move"
- **Information volume (volume) in answers** — The information volume of dialogue needs precise control: too little answer = suspicious (concealing); too much answer = suspicious (covering up); just the right amount = most suspicious (too perfect)

---

## IX. Transition Design

- **Default hard cuts** — Use hard cuts between shots within the same scene, maintaining the clean and tense rhythm of the game
- **Brief black screen between game rounds** — Between the end of one game round and the start of the next, a brief black screen of 0.3-0.5s can serve as a "breath," while suggesting "a new round has begun"
- **Hard cuts for perspective switches** — When switching from Character A's perspective to Character B's perspective, use hard cuts to create a "cognitive flip" impact
- **Flashbacks use quick flashes** — When a character suddenly recalls a key clue during a game, insert an extremely short flashback image (0.5-1s), simulating the cognitive experience of a "flash of insight"
- **Scene changes maintain tension** — Don't use soft transitions between different scenes; maintain the throughout tension of the psychological game. If a buffer is needed, use a short shot of the character alone (thinking / planning the next move) as an emotional transition
