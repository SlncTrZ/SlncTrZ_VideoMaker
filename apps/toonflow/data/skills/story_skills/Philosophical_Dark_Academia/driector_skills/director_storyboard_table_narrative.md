# Storyboard Table Narrative Techniques · Philosophical Dark Academia · Technique Reference

---

## I. Storyboard Table Positioning

The storyboard table is the director's tool for translating analytical rhythm into shot-by-shot timing and emphasis. Form fields are set by the director according to project needs (shot number, shot scale, camera movement, duration, subject, analytical intent, lighting, mood, transitions, etc.). The following only provides technique references under the philosophical dark academia narrative type.

---

## II. Shot Size Selection

- **Extreme Close-up is textual evidence** — Used on the object of analysis: the grain of paper, ink on parchment, a tear in a photograph, a single underlined word. The audience is invited to read alongside the narrator
- **Close-up is emotional reaction** — The thinker's face as an idea lands, the slight furrow of the brow during concentration, the widening of the eyes at a revelation
- **Medium shot is the thinker in context** — The narrator or subject surrounded by their intellectual environment: at a desk, before a bookshelf, in a lecture hall. Establishes the relationship between mind and world
- **Wide shot is atmospheric setting** — The library, the study, the cloister, the rain-streaked window. Wide shots define the intellectual container and give the mind room to wander
- **Macro shot is symbolic object** — A pressed flower, an old key, a wax seal, a lock of hair. Objects that carry metaphorical freight are given macro treatment, detached from narrative context, presented as pure symbol
- **Split-screen is comparative analysis** — Two texts, two images, two perspectives shown simultaneously. The audience performs the act of comparison. Avoid using split-screen for parallel action — use it for parallel thought

| Shot Size | Emotional Function | Duration Guide |
|---|---|---|
| Extreme Close-up | Evidence, revelation | 3-5s (reading time) |
| Close-up | Reaction, understanding | 3-4s |
| Medium | Presence, context | 4-6s |
| Wide | Atmosphere, scale | 5-8s |
| Macro | Symbolism, reverence | 4-6s |
| Split-screen | Comparison, synthesis | 6-10s |

---

## III. Camera Rhythm

- **Movement is intentional, never casual** — Every camera move serves the analytical argument. There is no "coverage" or "B-roll" — every frame is an argument
- **Stillness is the default** — 70% or more of shots should be static. The camera holds, waits, gives the audience time to think. Movement is reserved for shifts in the analytical direction
- **Panning is sequential reading** — A pan across a bookshelf, a timeline, a series of images. The camera enacts the act of scanning, listing, comparing. The speed equals the pace of thought
- **Push/pull is change in depth of analysis** — Push-in says "look closer, this matters." Pull-back says "see the larger context, this is part of something bigger"

---

## IV. Timing Control

- **Weight of silence** — After a major revelation, hold for 2-4 seconds of silence before the next word. The audience needs to feel the idea settle
- **Reading time** — When text is on screen (book page, letter, note), the duration must allow the audience to actually read it. Minimum 3 seconds for a sentence, 5-7 seconds for a paragraph
- **Analytical pause** — Between arguments, insert a 2-3 second beat of empty frame or atmospheric detail. This is the punctuation of intellectual rhythm
- **Emotional resonance duration** — After the synthesis moment, hold the final image for 6-10 seconds. Do not cut away. Let the audience sit with the completed thought. The fade to black should come only when the idea has been fully received
- **Extended holds for symbolic objects** — A candle burning, rain on glass, a portrait being studied — hold these 8-12 seconds. The audience's mind will wander to the meaning; that wandering is the point

---

## V. Shot Merging Strategy

- **Thematic clustering** — Group shots that serve the same analytical argument into a single sequence. Do not intercut between different lines of inquiry. Complete one thread before opening another
- **Symbolic juxtaposition** — Place shots of a symbolic object next to the analytical content it illuminates. The edit performs the act of connection for the audience
- **Argument layering** — Layer voiceover over b-roll that neither illustrates nor contradicts but deepens the frame. The relationship between what is heard and what is seen is where new meaning is generated

---

## VI. Transition Logic

| Transition | Emotional Function | When to Use |
|---|---|---|
| Fade to Black | Reflection, closure | End of major analytical segment, before a new inquiry begins |
| Dissolve | Connection, time passing | Between two thematically linked ideas, or across historical periods |
| Push-in | Emphasis, focusing | Transitioning from context to detail, from general to specific |
| Match Cut | Symbolic connection | Linking two visually similar but conceptually different subjects (e.g., a candle flame → a sunset) |
| Iris | Historical focus, intimacy | Highlighting a specific detail, simulating vintage lens, or narrowing to a single object |
| Whip Pan | Contrast, rupture | Sudden shift between opposing arguments or perspectives — the only "jarring" transition in the palette |
