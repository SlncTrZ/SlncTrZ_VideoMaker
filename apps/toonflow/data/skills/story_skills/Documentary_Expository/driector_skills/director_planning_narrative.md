# Narrative Technique · Documentary & Expository · Technique Reference

---

## I. Thematic Focus & Information Core

### Documentary & Expository Narrative Key Points

- **Objective truth anchor** — Every documentary must establish a clear relationship with truth. Whether observational, participatory, or expository, define the documentary stance upfront: who is speaking, what is their authority, and what evidence supports the thesis
- **Thesis-first approach** — Unlike fiction, documentary begins with a claim or question. The entire narrative structure serves to investigate, support, or challenge this central thesis. Every scene should answer "how does this serve the argument?"
- **Evidence hierarchy** — Primary sources (interviews, archival footage, data) take precedence over secondary interpretation. The director's role is to arrange evidence rather than invent it. Rank evidence by credibility within the narrative
- **Logical chain** — Information must flow in a traceable sequence: premise → evidence → analysis → conclusion. The audience should be able to follow the reasoning at every step. Break complex arguments into digestible links
- **Neutrality with purpose** — Documentary does not mean absence of point of view. It means transparency about point of view. Acknowledge counterarguments, present contradicting evidence, and let the audience weigh the facts
- **Data visualization as primary language** — Statistics, timelines, maps, and infographics are not supplementary — they are core narrative tools. Plan visual data representation in the same way a fiction director plans character acting

## II. Narrative Structure & Rhythm Planning

### Documentary & Expository Narrative Key Points

- **Structure types and their applications** —

| Structure Type | Description | Best Use Case |
|---|---|---|
| Problem-Solution | Present an issue → analyze causes → propose resolution | Social issues, policy docs, investigative |
| Chronological Timeline | Events unfold in temporal order, building context progressively | Historical docs, biography, event recap |
| Compare-Contrast | Juxtapose two subjects, periods, or perspectives to reveal insights | Cultural comparison, scientific before/after |
| Cause-Effect | Trace a chain of causation from origin to consequence | Science explainers, environmental docs |
| Pyramid / Inverted | Most important fact first → supporting details → background | News-style, summarization, quick explainers |

- **Information density curve** — Unlike fiction's emotional arc, documentary follows an "information density arc": introduce core concept at low density → layer complexity progressively → reach peak information density at analysis midpoint → descend to synthesis and conclusion. - **Pacing through section weight** — Not all sections carry equal information weight. Alternate heavy data segments with "breathing sections" (case studies, human stories, visual sequences) that let the audience process without new information
- **Narrative signposting** — Use clear structural markers to guide the audience: "Chapter 1: The Problem," visual chapter cards, recurring graphic motifs that signal topic shifts. Documentary audiences need wayfinding
- **Cold open thesis** — The first 30-60 seconds must establish the central question or stakes. Unlike fiction's slow build, documentary audiences decide commitment early. Lead with the most compelling evidence or question

## III. Scene-by-Scene Evidence Design

### Documentary & Expository Narrative Key Points

- **Describe evidence not emotion** — Don't say "sad," say "archival footage of empty factory, employment graph declining 2004-2010, voiceover citing 12,000 lost jobs." The facts carry the emotion. Concrete evidence evokes more response than manufactured sentiment
- **Typical documentary scene types and design** —

| Scene Type | Information Function | Narrative Technique | Visual Approach |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hook / Opening | Establish thesis, capture attention | Cold open with strongest stat or question | High-impact archival or data visualization |
| Data Visualization | Present evidence visually | Animated charts, maps, infographic sequences | 2.5D parallax, kinetic typography, motion graphics |
| Expert Interview | Provide authority and interpretation | Talking head with credential establishing shot | Medium close-up, subtle push-in for emphasis |
| Case Study | Humanize abstract information | Follow one subject as representative example | Cinematic verité, natural lighting, ambient sound |
| Timeline Progression | Show change over time | Chronological sequence with visual markers | Parallax slides, map zooms, date transitions |
| Conclusion / CTA | Synthesize argument, call to action | Recap evidence stack, present resolution | Montage of key visuals, music swell to end |

- **Evidence pacing rule** — Present no more than three new data points per minute. The documentary audience needs processing time. Information overload causes disengagement faster than slow pacing
- **The "Case Study Sandwich"** — Bracket data segments with human stories: Human story (emotional entry) → Data/analysis → Return to human story (emotional resolution). This structure makes statistics memorable
- **Archival integration** — Archival footage should be contextualized: date stamp, source credit, or juxtaposition with present-day equivalent. Never leave the audience wondering "when was this filmed?"

## IV. Sound & Music Direction

### Documentary & Expository Narrative Key Points

- **Credibility scoring** — Documentary music must never sound manipulative. Choose instruments and arrangements that suggest objectivity and authority rather than manufactured emotion. The score supports, not directs, the audience's response
- **Instrument selection model** —

| Instrument Category | Credibility Level | Emotional Weight | Documentary Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| Strings (solo cello/violin) | Medium | High | Human stories, emotional case studies |
| Piano (minimalist) | High | Medium | Data segments, analysis sections |
| Ambient / Drone | Highest | Low | Neutral narration, timeline sequences |
| Brass (distant/solo) | Low | High | Heroic or tragic turning points — use sparingly |
| Percussion (subtle) | High | Low | Pacing markers, data transitions |

- **Environmental sound as primary texture** — Documentary's most powerful audio tool is real-world sound: factory machinery, forest ambience, city traffic, crowd murmur. These sounds transport the audience to the subject's world more effectively than any score
- **Voiceover tone** — Expository voiceover must be measured, clear, and authoritative without being didactic. Avoid overly emotional delivery. The information speaks for itself; the narrator is a guide, not a performer
- **Syncing sound to data** — When presenting data, synchronize sonic markers (subtle tones, clicks, percussion hits) to data points appearing on screen. This audiovisual sync creates a "scientific" feel that reinforces credibility
- **Silence for gravity** — Leave moments of complete silence after significant revelations, giving the audience space to absorb. In documentary, silence is not absence of sound — it is the sound of thinking

## V. Composition & Visual Style

### Documentary & Expository Narrative Key Points

- **2.5D parallax as primary motion technique** — For still-image-based documentary (historical photos, archival documents), use 2.5D parallax: separate image into depth layers (foreground/midground/background) and move each at different speeds. Creates depth and cinematic movement from static materials
- **Kinetic typography for emphasis** — Key statistics, dates, and quotes should appear as animated text on screen. Use restrained motion (fade in/count-up/typewriter) rather than flashy effects. The typography serves the information, not decoration
- **Data visualization styles** —
  - **Animated line graphs** — Best for trends over time
  - **Proportional area charts** — Best for comparisons of magnitude
  - **Geographic maps with data pins** — Best for location-based information
  - **Timeline bars** — Best for chronological sequences
  - **Split-screen comparison** — Best for before/after or contrast subjects
- **Archival footage treatment** — Apply subtle film grain, slight desaturation, or frame-rate adjustment to distinguish archival from contemporary footage. The visual difference signals "this is from the past" without text labels
- **Re-enactment visual language** — If using dramatized re-enactments, differentiate them stylistically: shallow depth of field, desaturated color, or vignette. The audience should never confuse re-enactment with documentary reality
- **Lower-third discipline** — All on-screen text names, dates, locations follow a consistent lower-third template. Standardize font, size, position, and animation. Consistency builds professional credibility

## VI. Camera Movement & Rhythm

### Documentary & Expository Narrative Key Points

- **Controlled motion is the default** — Documentary camera work should be deliberate and restrained. Pan and zoom only with purpose — to reveal information, follow a subject, or emphasize a detail. Random camera movement undermines credibility
- **Slow push-in for emphasis** — Slowly push in on a subject (photograph, document, data point, interviewee) to signal "this matters." Push-in duration: 3-5 seconds for subtle emphasis, 6-8 seconds for significant reveals. Never rush
- **Slow pull-out for context** — Pull out from a close detail to reveal its wider context — from a date on a document to the full document, from a face in a crowd to the whole crowd. Pull-outs answer "where does this fit?"
- **Parallax depth transitions** — Transition between scenes using 2.5D parallax movement: push through one image layer to reveal the next scene behind it. Creates a "moving through information space" feeling appropriate for documentary continuity
- **Static-to-dynamic pacing pattern** — Open each section with a static frame (establishing context) → introduce slow motion (building engagement) → reach dynamic moment (data reveal or key evidence) → return to static (let audience absorb). This wave pattern manages cognitive flow
- **Interview camera discipline** — Interview subjects framed at medium close-up, eyeline to interviewer (not camera), subtle push-in during key answers. Camera must be locked off on tripod — no handheld during interview segments. Stability signals respect and credibility
- **B-roll as evidence, not filler** — Every B-roll shot should carry information: a machine operating, a landscape changing, hands working, documents being signed. B-roll in documentary is not decoration — it is supporting evidence for the narration
