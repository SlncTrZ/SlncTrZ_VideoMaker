# Storyboard Table Narrative Techniques · Documentary & Expository · Technique Reference

---

## I. Storyboard Table Positioning

The storyboard table is the director's core tool for translating scripts into camera language. Form fields are set by the director based on project needs (shot number, shot size, camera movement, duration, characters, events, dialogue, lighting, mood, transitions, etc.). In documentary, the table prioritizes information delivery over dramatic tension — every shot decision answers "what does the audience learn here?"

---

## II. Shot Size Selection

- **Wide / Establishing shot** — Provides geographic and temporal context: "where are we, what year, what is the scale." Essential at the start of each section to orient the audience. Duration: 3-5s for full contextual absorption
- **Medium shot** — Primary carrier of narration. Talking head interviews, narrator on camera, subject demonstrating a process. Maintains connection while preserving enough background for context. Duration: tied to information delivery — hold until a complete thought is delivered
- **Close-up** — Reserves for evidence: a document being signed, hands assembling a device, a detail in archival footage. Close-ups signal "pay attention to this specific piece of information." Duration: 2-3s for detail scanning, 4-5s if text must be read
- **Infographic / Data shot** — Full-screen data visualization (charts, maps, timelines, diagrams). Pure information delivery mode. Duration: minimum 4s per data point, longer for multi-variable graphics. Narration sync determines exact length
- **Archival / Historical shot** — Footage or stills from the time period being discussed. Visually signals "this is evidence from the past." Treatment: subtle film grain or frame-rate adjustment in post-production

---

## III. Camera Rhythm

- **Movement must have information purpose** — Every camera move (pan, tilt, push, pull) must directly serve revealing information. Unmotivated movement breaks the documentary contract of "every frame is intentional"
- **Locked-off credibility** — Documentary's default camera state is stable and locked. Tripod shots communicate authority. Handheld is reserved for verité segments or situations where stability is impossible (active conflict, documentary of happening)
- **Parallax as signature movement** — For still-image-heavy content, the primary camera movement is 2.5D parallax push/pull through layered images. This movement creates cinematic depth while honoring the static nature of the source material
- **Data emphasis stillness** — When a data point appears on screen, the camera must be completely still. Movement during data presentation distracts from information absorption. The data speaks; the camera listens

---

## IV. Timing Control

- **Data hold time** — Every data visualization needs minimum 4s of screen time for the audience to register, plus 2s per additional variable. Rushing data is the most common documentary storyboard mistake
- **Narration sync** — Shot duration is primarily determined by voiceover length, not visual aesthetics. A shot can be held longer than visually comfortable if the narration demands it. Print the narration line in the shot field
- **Parallax transition pacing** — Parallax movements (pushing through layers) need 2-3s for the transition itself. The audience needs time to process "we are moving between information spaces." Rushed parallax feels disorienting
- **Emphasis duration** — When the narration says "this is the key finding," the corresponding shot should hold at least 2s after the line ends. This post-narration hold gives the audience space to absorb significance
- **Break shot every 90 seconds** — Insert a wider shot or context-establishing shot every 90s to prevent information fatigue. These breaks can be as short as 2s but are mandatory for cognitive reset

---

## V. Shot Merging Strategy

- **Information chunking** — Group related data points into a single shot sequence. For example: map establishing where → timeline showing when → graph showing how much → all in one continuous camera move or matched cut sequence. Chunking prevents the "firehose of disconnected facts" effect
- **Evidence grouping** — Merge archival footage, interview response, and data visualization about the same topic into a single scene block. The evidence pileup confirms the thesis from multiple angles before moving on
- **Thematic clustering** — Within each chapter, cluster shots by thematic unit rather than source type. Don't separate "all interviews" from "all data." Mix them within thematic clusters so each mini-argument is self-contained
- **Director's mindset check** — After writing the storyboard, self-check: Can the audience draw the conclusion without the narrator spelling it out? (yes = correct). Is every shot carrying information? (yes = correct). Are data shots given enough time? (yes = correct)

---

## VI. Transition Logic

| Transition | Effect | Documentary Use |
|---|---|---|
| Cut | Direct, invisible, fastest | Default transition for within-section shots. Keeps information flow tight |
| Dissolve | Gentle passage of time | Time passage within same location, or between interview and corresponding B-roll |
| Parallax Slide | Move through information layers | Topic shift within same chapter. Push through one evidence layer to reveal next |
| Data-Driven Wipe | Graphical wipe shaped by chart/map | Statistical pivot or geographic shift. Gives the transition itself information value |
| Archival Fade | Fade through grainy film texture | Entry or exit from historical footage. Signals "we are entering/leaving the past" |
| Hard Cut | Abrupt, no transition | Emphasis — cutting from evidence to conclusion or from analysis to human story. Disruption signals "pay attention to this contrast" |
