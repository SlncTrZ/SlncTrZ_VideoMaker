# Storyboard Table Narrative Techniques · Historical Epic · Technique Reference

---

## 1. Storyboard Positioning

The storyboard is the director's core tool for translating the script into visual language. The form fields (shot number, shot size, camera movement, duration, characters, action, dialogue, lighting, emotion, transition, etc.) are set by the director according to project needs. The following only provides technique references for the historical epic narrative genre.

---

## 2. Shot Size Selection

- **Long shots are the breath of epic** — In epic narrative, long shots/extreme long shots should be used more frequently than in other genres. Long shots convey "the vastness of the era and the insignificance of the individual," which is the core source of the epic feel
- **Close-ups are the human anchor** — After every grand long shot sequence, arrange at least one character close-up as an emotional anchor. The jump from "heaven and earth to eyes" is a signature technique of epic narrative
- **Medium shots carry ensemble scenes** — Many multi-character dialogues, court confrontations, and war council scenes in epic require medium shots to establish character relationships and spatial positioning
- **Extreme close-ups for fateful moments** — Extreme close-ups (eyes/hands/keepsakes) are reserved for "turning points of fate": hands receiving an imperial edict, eyes before making a decision, a keepsake being broken
- **Establishing shots must have epic feel** — Establishing shots should not only set the space but convey "what kind of era this is" — a desolate mountain pass is more narratively powerful than a caption reading "xx City"

---

## 3. Camera Movement Rhythm

- **Default slowness** — In epic narrative, camera movement speed should be universally slowed down, with over 70% of shots being static or in very slow motion. Hurried camera work is incompatible with epic's solemn temperament
- **Slow push-in = fate focusing** — "History's gaze focuses on this person/this moment," suitable for key decisions, heroic moments
- **Slow pull-out = revealing the full picture** — Slowly pulling back from an individual to reveal a grand panorama (battlefield/ruins/crowd) is a classic epic camera movement from "individual fate to the tide of the era"
- **Overlooking panning = scroll of the era** — High-angle slow panning to display grand scenes, like unrolling a historical scroll
- **Still gaze = historical witness** — At key historical moments, the camera should be absolutely still, like a witness gazing upon history. Stillness itself is the most solemn "camera movement"

---

## 4. Timing Control

- **Long shots/extreme long shots 5-7s** — Epic long shots need enough duration for the audience to feel the vastness of space and the weight of history. Don't cut away hastily
- **Confrontation/dialogue close-ups 4-5s** — The rhythm of dialogue in epic is slower than in everyday genres. Leave silent space between each line, giving words the weight to "land"
- **Lengthen ritual segments** — In ritual scenes (kneeling/oath-taking/sacrifice), every action should be given sufficient duration (3-5s/action). The solemnity of ritual comes from "not being in a hurry"
- **Close-ups 3-4s** — Close-ups in epic carry "moments of fate" and need longer lingering than in everyday genres for the audience to feel the emotional weight
- **Don't shorten battle segments** — Although battle scenes quicken the pace, individual shot durations should not be drastically compressed. Use shot size changes rather than shortening duration to create tension
- **Golden rule: solemnity comes from not being in a hurry** — Every rhythm in epic narrative should be "half a beat slower" than everyday genres. Haste = frivolity, ease = solemnity

---

## 5. Shot Merging Strategy (De-AI-ification)

- **Cover ensemble scenes in one take** — Multi-person scenes like court/war council/banquets that can be covered with one camera movement (slow pan across the group) should not be broken into individual fragmentary shots
- **Merge marching/migration** — Moving scenes like marching columns or civilian migrations should be merged into one continuous camera movement. Chopping them up destroys the sense of "flowing torrent"
- **Narrative density first** — Every shot must advance the narrative or convey epic emotion. Purely displaying environmental shots should be merged into shots with narrative function
- **Director's thinking check** — After writing the storyboard, self-check: if an epic film director would use one slow long take to complete a segment, don't break it into fragments

---

## 6. One-Shot Sequences (Long Take Merging)

- **Applicable conditions** — Battlefield traversal, full ritual process, characters crossing space from one state to another. The value of long takes in epic narrative is "letting the audience exist alongside history"
- **Typical scenes** — A character walking through post-war ruins, the entire process of an army setting out, ritual procession from the palace gate to the main hall, traversal across the battlefield from one end to the other
- **How to annotate** — Write the complete path in the camera movement field (e.g., "One-shot: overlooking the army departing → slow push-in descending to the protagonist's side → tracking alongside the protagonist → final frame: protagonist looks back at the city gate"), and describe the opening and closing frames in the visual description
- **Relaxed duration limits** — Epic long takes can exceed the 6s single-shot limit but should not exceed 15s, as information is continuously updated and immersion is needed
- **Solemnity first** — The movement speed of long takes should always remain solemn and slow, even in battle scenes it should not be rushed

---

## 7. Character Interaction Design

- **Confrontation is epic's home ground** — The most tense interactions in epic narrative are not fights but confrontations: the silence of two people staring at each other, undercurrents at the negotiation table, probing between ruler and subject
- **Individuals within the ensemble** — In ensemble interaction scenes, there should be an emotional thread running through a "focal character." The ensemble is the background; the individual's reaction is the narrative anchor
- **Body language as power** — Character interaction in epic relies heavily on body language to convey power relations: standing vs. kneeling, front vs. back, high vs. low, facing vs. turning away

---

## 8. Dialogue and Silence

- **Epic dialogue should be short** — A single word that carries weight is more powerful than a long speech. Key lines should be kept within one sentence, giving words the weight of being "carved in stone"
- **Silence is epic's punctuation** — Sufficient silence must be left before and after key lines. Silence lets the audience "hear" the weight of the words
- **Use narration sparingly** — Epic narrative should avoid using narration to explain historical background. Use images, space, and character reactions to convey information — more convincing than narration

---

## 9. Transition Design

- **Use empty shots for scene transitions** — Insert an epic empty shot (flag, city wall, sky, wilderness) between different scenes as an emotional buffer while conveying the texture of the era
- **Use imagery transitions for time spans** — To express the passage of time, don't use date captions; use image dissolves: fresh snow covering an old battlefield, a seedling growing into a tree, a new person sitting in the old person's position
- **Use fade to black/fade in for segment transitions** — Between major segments, use slow fade to black → fade in transitions, simulating the ritual feel of "turning a page of history"
- **Default hard cuts** — Use hard cuts between shots within the same scene to keep the narrative clean and solemn
