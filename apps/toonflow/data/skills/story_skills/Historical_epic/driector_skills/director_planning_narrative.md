# Narrative Techniques · Historical Epic · Technique Reference

---

## 1. Thematic Focus & Emotional Core

### Historical Epic Narrative Key Points

- **Small People in Big Times** — The core of epic narrative is not to recreate historical events but to show the fate of individuals in the torrent of the era. The thematic focus should center on "how one person's choice reflects an entire era"
- **The Irresistible Force of Fate** — The tension of historical narrative comes from "the collision of individual will with the tide of the era." The harder a character struggles, the more it reveals the cruelty of fate and the greatness of humanity
- **Ritual as Weight** — Key historical moments (coronation, oath-taking, decisive battle, final parting, martyrdom) must be given ritual. Ritual is not about grandeur but about every action and every word carrying the solemnity of "irreversibility"
- **Suggested audience takeaway** — Desolation / sublime beauty / wistfulness / awe. Avoid frivolous directions like "thrilling" or "satisfying"
- **Echoes of history** — Good epic narrative makes the audience feel that "this didn't only happen in that era." Use shared human dilemmas across time (conflict between loyalty and righteousness, individual vs. collective) to create cross-era resonance

## 2. Narrative Structure & Rhythm Planning

### Historical Epic Narrative Key Points

- **Macro-micro interweaving is the fundamental skill** — Epic narrative needs to constantly switch between "macro panorama" and "micro individual." Pure macro becomes empty; pure micro loses the epic feel. Every grand segment needs an individual emotional anchor
- **Epic rhythm arc** — Epic's rhythm is not steady forward but a large wave of "accumulation → eruption → settling." Long periods of foreshadowing and accumulation earn one soul-stirring eruption, then silence to digest the aftershocks
- **Ensemble narrative** — Epic typically involves multiple character threads. Each thread should have its own emotional arc, converging at key nodes. The convergence point is the epic climax
- **Handling time spans** — Historical narrative often spans years or decades. Use "time-marking events" rather than "date captions" to convey the passage of time: white hair, new grass on ruins, a child growing up
- **Turning points use silence, not speeches** — The handling of historical turning points should prioritize silent means (eye contact, sitting alone, gazing into the distance) over impassioned rhetoric. True historical decisions are often made in silence
- **The "last time" model** — The poignant moments in epic narrative are often "the last time": the last look back at one's old city, the last toast, the last battle fought side by side. Mark each character's "last time" node during narrative planning

## 3. Scene-by-Scene Emotion Design

### Historical Epic Narrative Key Points

- **Use epic vocabulary for emotional goals** — Don't say "sad," say "the desolation of watching an era come to an end." Epic emotion needs to transcend the personal level, carrying a sense of the era and fate
- **Typical emotional segments and design** —

| Segment Type | Emotional Direction | Narrative Technique | Music Suggestion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Peaceful prosperity | Flourishing + undercurrents | Display order and prosperity while planting seeds of impending collapse | Solemn instruments with undercurrents beneath |
| Sudden upheaval | Shock + powerlessness | Chain reaction of collapsing order, individuals swept along | Sudden silence followed by low-frequency pressure |
| Dilemma/choice | Agony + resolve | Character struggling between loyalty and righteousness, life and death, ultimately making a choice | Single instrument solo, minimal |
| Decisive battle/confrontation | Sublime + tragic | Doing what one knows is impossible, expressing resolve through action rather than words | From silence to gradually swelling instruments |
| Cost/sacrifice | Pain + awe | The cost of victory, the final moments of the fallen | Choral voices or pure ambient sound |
| Dust settles | Desolation + release | Silence after everything ends, the survivor looking back | Minimal instruments or wind sounds |

- **"Power space" is a core narrative tool** — Use spatial relationships to map power relations and fateful situations:
  - **High position**: looking down = wielding power/judging, suitable for emperors, decision-makers
  - **Low position**: looking up = submission/insignificance, suitable for individuals at the mercy of fate
  - **Eye level**: equality/confrontation, suitable for key dialogues between equals
- **Space as era** — Use changes in space to convey the passage of time: a once-prosperous palace reduced to ruins, a battlefield overgrown with weeds, an empty throne. Space itself is the most powerful narrative of time
- **Narrative requirements for ritual scenes** — Every action in ritual segments (coronation/oath-taking/final parting) must have narrative meaning: kneeling = submission and sacrifice; raising a glass = a pact of life and death; turning away = no turning back. Don't let rituals become empty pageantry

## 4. Sound & Music Direction

### Historical Epic Narrative Key Points

- **Heavy bass is the epic foundation** — The base of epic scoring is the weight of the low register (cellos, timpani, low brass). The high register is reserved for key emotional eruption points; everyday segments rely on bass to support the "weight of history"
- **Power of the human voice** — In epic narrative, the human voice (chorus, chanting, work songs) has a special narrative function: war songs of departing armies, dirges of farewell, silent chorus after victory. The human voice conveys "the fate of the collective" more powerfully than instruments
- **Solemn silence** — At key historical moments (the final decision on the throne, the gaze before execution, the moment after gunfire ceases on the battlefield), all music should be removed, leaving only ambient sound or pure silence. Silence gives these moments the weight of "history happening now"
- **Ambient sound carries the texture of the era** — Each scene should mark core ambient sounds to convey the atmosphere of the era: the sound of iron hooves, flags flapping in the wind, distant horns, market bustle, wind through ruins
- **Music emotional progression model** —

| Emotional Stage | Music Strategy | Coverage |
|---|---|---|
|Foreshadowing/everyday | Low instrument background, solemn but restrained | Medium-low |
| Undercurrent/accumulation | Drums gradually entering, rhythm suggesting fate approaching | Medium |
| Eruption/decisive battle | Full orchestration or sudden complete silence | Extreme |
| Sacrifice/cost | Choral voices or single instrument lament | Medium-high |
| Dust settles | Minimal instruments or pure ambient sound (wind) | Low |

- **The battlefield is not noise** — The sound design of battle segments should not be chaotic noise of indiscriminate fighting but layered soundscapes: close-range breathing and weapon clashes, mid-range battle cries, distant horns and hoofbeats. Layering is narrative

## 5. Composition & Shot Size Narrative

### Historical Epic Narrative Key Points

- **Narrative functions of three core compositions** —
  - **Symmetrical composition** — Solemnity/power/ritual/inviolable order, suitable for court, hall, and confrontation scenes
  - **Rule of thirds** — Everyday/marching/dialogue, stable and balanced, suitable for narrative progression segments
  - **Large areas of negative space** — Desolation/insignificance/emptiness of fate, the solitude of the individual between heaven and earth
- **Epic value of macro long shots** — Long shots and extreme long shots are the primary shot sizes of epic narrative. Long shots are not filler but "letting the audience feel the vastness of the era and the insignificance of the individual." Give long shots sufficient duration
- **Close-ups as human anchors** — Cutting from a grand long shot to a close-up of a character's eyes is the core technique of "returning from the era to the individual." The greater the contrast from long shot to close-up, the stronger the emotional impact
- **Spatial depth as power** — Positional relationships within frame depth suggest power relations: the person standing furthest away is the most powerful (stronger when facing away from the camera); the person kneeling in the foreground is the most humble
- **Ensemble composition** — Epic often needs to accommodate multiple characters in one frame. The core of ensemble composition is "highlighting the individual within the group" — make the main character identifiable through position, posture, and contrast of motion vs. stillness
- **Solitary individual and vast space** — A person standing alone in an empty battlefield/ruins/great hall — the smaller the character's proportion in the frame, the greater the sense of solitude and the weight of history

## 6. Camera Movement & Rhythm

### Historical Epic Narrative Key Points

- **Solemn slow movement as default** — Camera movement in epic narrative should proceed at an extremely slow pace, conveying "the heavy footsteps of history." Hurried techniques like quick push-ins and fast cuts are incompatible with epic temperament
- **Overlooking = perspective of fate** — High-angle overlooking is a signature epic camera movement, suggesting "fate/history looking down upon mortals." Suitable for battlefield panorama, city falls, mass migrations
- **Low-angle shot = reverence/oppression** — Low-angle shots are suitable for a hero's last stand, monuments, city walls. The steeper the low angle, the stronger the sense of reverence or oppression
- **Still gaze** — At key historical moments (final parting/coronation/before the final battle), the camera should be absolutely still, like a historical witness gazing. Any movement would destroy this "time frozen" solemnity
- **Slow pan = revealing the full picture/scroll of the era** — Slow horizontal panning is suitable for displaying grand scenes: army assembly, city panorama, post-war ruins. The speed of the slow pan should allow the audience time to "read" every detail in the frame
- **Camera movement as narrative perspective** — Overlooking = perspective of fate (calm, omniscient); tracking parallel to the character = walking alongside them (empathy); slow push-in = focusing key point (emphasis); slow pull-out = revealing the big picture (impact)
