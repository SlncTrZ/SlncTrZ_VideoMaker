# Narrative Technique · Sweet Romance · Technique Reference

---

## I. Thematic Conception & Emotional Core

### Sweet Romance Narrative Points

- **Subtlety and restraint first** — Emotional expression relies not on dialogue exposition but on blank spaces and subtle reactions. Thematic conception should lean toward restraint and subtlety, avoiding overt sentimentality
- **Controlled sweetness** — "Almost touching" is more effective than "stuck together." The main emotional thread should design a push-pull rhythm of "wants to say but doesn't." The sweetness comes from what the audience fills in with their own imagination
- **Small details, big impact** — Don't pursue grand-scale emotional impact. Move people with details: a glance, a hesitation to speak, a collar blown askew by the wind
- **Suggested audience takeaway directions** — Heartache / lingering regret / heart-fluttering / healing. Avoid directions that mismatch the sweet romance temperament like "thrill," "passion," or "exhilaration"
- **Warmth within coolness, intimacy within distance** — Sweet romance doesn't mean cloying. The overall tone can lean cooler, more distant, but release warmth at key nodes — contrast is the greatest sweetness

---

## II. Narrative Structure & Rhythm Planning

### Sweet Romance Narrative Points

- **Slow is the fundamental skill** — Sweet romance has high density of emotional information (glances, subtle expressions, physical distance) that requires time for the audience to "feel." The overall pace leans slow, but not dragging — every segment carries emotional increment
- **Emotional curve should be gradual** — Avoid "flat flat flat → sudden explosion." Use progressive emotional escalation, each segment one level more emotionally intense than the last
- **Turning points use action, not dialogue** — At key turning points, prioritize action means (change in gaze, change in physical distance, silence, object delivery) over relying on dialogue explanation
- **Use emotional buffering between segments** — Segment transitions need emotional buffering, not hard cuts. Use environmental empty shots, solitude fragments, or daily life snippets as breathing space
- **"Fast" in climax segments isn't about editing speed** — It's about emotional density. Use tighter shot scale switches (full body → medium → close-up → extreme close-up) to create a racing-heart sensation, rather than shortening dwell time
- **Push-pull rhythm model** — The core engine of sweet romance is "push-pull": approach → retreat → approach again → misunderstanding → separation → reunion. Each round of push-pull should be deeper, more painful, and sweeter than the last

## III. Scene-by-Scene Emotional Design

### Sweet Romance Narrative Points

- **Emotional goals use concrete words** — Don't say "happy," say "can't suppress the smile after a secret heart-flutter." Concrete emotional descriptions better guide shot scale selection and performance details
- **Typical emotional scene types and design** —

| Scene Type | Emotional Direction | Narrative Technique | Music Suggestion |
|---|---|---|---|
| First Meeting / Appearance | Dazzled + Curiosity | From a bystander's perspective "discovering" the other, from far to near | Blank space, only environmental sound creating a "breath-holding" feel |
| Everyday Secret Crush | Undercurrent + Restraint | Sneaking glances, hesitating to speak, deliberately keeping distance | Soft instrumental, low volume, background |
| Misunderstanding / Separation | Heartache + Endurance | Not explaining, turning away, crying alone | Sorrowful solo, or pure environmental sound |
| Confession / Reconciliation | Relief + Heart-fluttering | Speaking after silence, eyes before words | From quiet to warm instrumental, gradual fade-in |
| Escalation / Ambiguity | Nervous + Sweet | Physical distance shortening, light touch, breath audible | Light rhythmic beat, implying heartbeat |
| High Sweetness / Wedding | Happiness + Solemnity | Ritualistic, solemn eye contact, promises | Full instrumental, solemn but gentle |

- **"Distance" is the core narrative tool** — Use the physical distance between characters to map relationship progress:
  - **Early stage**: Wide shot / half body, large physical distance, polite speech
  - **Middle stage**: Medium shot, distance shortened but with barriers (objects / crowds / hesitation)
  - **Late stage**: Close-up / extreme close-up, zero distance, all psychological defenses down
- **Spatial elements as emotional metaphor** — Use scene elements to convey emotion, reducing reliance on dialogue. For example: a blurred figure behind a curtain = estrangement; opening a door to a courtyard full of blooming flowers = relief; sitting alone in the rain = loneliness
- **Shot intent should write "why" not "how to shoot"** — "Using a close-up to let the audience see the hesitation in her eyes" is better than "using a close-up to film her face." When the intent is clear, the storyboard naturally selects the right shot scale and angle

## IV. Sound & Music Direction

### Sweet Romance Narrative Points

- **Silence is more powerful than music** — At key emotional moments (eye contact, tears falling, turning to leave), prioritize removing music, leaving only environmental sound. The "sweetness" of sweet romance often comes from what the audience fills in after the silence
- **Music mood follows segments** — Don't score scene by scene. Divide by segments and set one musical mood foundation for each. Within the same segment, scene transitions rely on environmental sound changes, not frequent song switches
- **Avoid full coverage** — Overall music coverage should not exceed 60%. The "silence" of blank segments forms a breathing rhythm with the scored segments
- **Environmental sound is half the atmosphere** — Mark 1-2 core environmental sounds per scene to assist subsequent sound design. The richer the layers of environmental sound, the more immersive the scene
- **Music emotion progression model** —

| Emotional Stage | Music Strategy | Coverage |
|---|---|---|
| Stable / Daily | Soft instrumental background | Low |
| Undercurrent / Brewing | Single instrument solo, very low volume | Low-Medium |
| Emotional Outburst | Instrumental gradually fuller or sudden silence | Medium-High |
| Fateful Turning Point | Intense instrumental or complete silence | Extreme |
| Re-warming / Healing | Warm instrumental slow fade-in | Medium |

- **Sweet romance's "heartbeat feel"** — In ambiguous/escalation segments, use light rhythmic percussion (hand drum, wood block, plucked strings) to suggest heartbeat acceleration, which is more sophisticated than using sweet melodies directly

## V. Composition & Shot Scale Narrative

### Sweet Romance Narrative Points

- **Three core compositions and their narrative functions** —
  - **Generous negative space** — Loneliness / mood / poetic space, conveying the character's psychological isolation or emotional blankness
  - **Framing composition** — Figures behind gauze curtains / door frames / window lattices / screens, creating a "sneaking a look" secret-admire perspective and a sense of estrangement
  - **Rule of thirds** — Dialogue / daily life / two-person interaction, stable and balanced, suitable for everyday sweet segments
- **Center composition is reserved** — Center composition is reserved for formal appearances and ritualistic scenes (e.g., wedding, formal confession). Not used in daily scenes, or it loses its ceremonial impact
- **Spatial depth as narrative** — Foreground obstruction (curtain / flower branch / mist) + medium shot subject + background environment: the more layers, the stronger the sense of estrangement; the fewer layers, the more intimate
- **Vertical vs. horizontal composition** — Single-person close-ups / appearances lean toward vertical (emphasizing loneliness and physique); two-person / scenes lean toward horizontal (emphasizing relationship and shared space)
- **Sweet romance shot scale progression** — Within the same scene, shot scales should progress with emotional rising: half body → medium → close-up → extreme close-up. Don't jump straight to extreme close-ups — leave room for emotional ascent
- **Extreme close-ups need a reason** — Extreme close-ups (eyes / lips / hands) are emotional nukes. 2-3 per episode is enough. Overuse fatigues the audience
- **Wide shots are not transitions** — Wide shots have their own narrative value (loneliness, spatial oppression, seasonal atmosphere). Give wide shots enough duration (4-6s), don't rush to cut away

## VI. Camera Movement & Rhythm

### Sweet Romance Narrative Points

- **Static as the default** — Over 60% of shots should be from static camera positions, letting frame details and emotion speak for themselves
- **Slow push = approach / heart-flutter** — The psychological suggestion of "the audience moving closer to the character," suitable for heart-fluttering, discovery, peering
- **Slow pull = withdrawal / loneliness** — The psychological suggestion of "the audience stepping back," suitable for parting, loneliness, revealing the full picture
- **Quick cuts don't fit** — Fast editing is incompatible with the sweet romance temperament. Even in climax segments, build rhythm through shot scale progression, not quick cuts
- **Panning and tracking** — Slow pans are suitable for revealing the full picture of a scene or following a character's walk; tracking shots are suitable for ceremonial / walking scenes. All speeds should remain restrained
- **Camera movement is emotion** — Camera movement is not a technical choice but an emotional one. Static = calm / suppressed; slow push = approach / heart-flutter; slow pull = withdrawal / loneliness; slow pan = display / solemnity
- **Sweet romance "heartbeat camera movement"** — In ambiguous escalation segments, use subtle slow pushes paired with shot scale progression (half body → medium → close-up), simulating the physiological feeling of "narrowing attention" as the heartbeat accelerates
