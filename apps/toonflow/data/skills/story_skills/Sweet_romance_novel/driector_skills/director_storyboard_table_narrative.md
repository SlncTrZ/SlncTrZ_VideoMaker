# Storyboard Table Narrative Technique · Sweet Romance · Technique Reference

---

## I. Storyboard Table Positioning

The storyboard table is the director's core tool for converting script into camera language. Table fields are defined by the director according to project needs (shot number, shot scale, camera movement, duration, characters, events, dialogue, lighting, emotion, transitions, etc.). The following provides technique references specific to the sweet romance narrative type.

---

## II. Shot Scale Selection

- **Sweet romance shot scale progression** — Within the same scene, shot scales should progress with emotional rising: half body → medium → close-up → extreme close-up. Don't jump straight to extreme close-ups — leave room for emotional ascent
- **Wide shots are not transitions** — Wide shots have their own narrative value (loneliness, spatial oppression, seasonal atmosphere). Give wide shots enough duration (4-6s), don't rush to cut away
- **Extreme close-ups need a reason** — Extreme close-ups (eyes / lips / hands) are emotional nukes. 2-3 per episode is enough. Overuse fatigues the audience
- **Establishing shots should be concise** — Establishing shots (establishing) at most 1-2 shots to get it done. Don't break into 3+ fragments. Typical approach: 1 extreme wide / wide establishing shot + 1 full shot introducing the subject, or directly 1 wide shot with slow push to complete establishing + introduction. Avoid the redundant three-part formula of "shoot environment first → then shoot details → then shoot character arriving"

---

## III. Camera Movement Rhythm

- **Static by default** — Over 60% of shots should be from static camera positions, letting frame details and emotion speak for themselves
- **Slow push = emotional progression** — The psychological suggestion of "the audience moving closer to the character," suitable for heart-fluttering, discovery, peering
- **Slow pull = emotional withdrawal** — The psychological suggestion of "the audience stepping back," suitable for parting, loneliness, revealing the full picture
- **Camera movement is emotion** — Camera movement is not a technical choice but an emotional one. Static = calm / suppressed; slow push = approach / heart-flutter; slow pull = withdrawal / loneliness; slow pan = display / solemnity
- **Sweet romance "heartbeat camera movement"** — In ambiguous escalation segments, use subtle slow pushes paired with shot scale progression (half body → medium → close-up), simulating the physiological feeling of "narrowing attention" as the heartbeat accelerates

---

## IV. Duration Control

- **Close-up / expression shots** — 2-3s, focusing on subtle expression changes
- **Dialogue medium shots** — 3-4s, steady delivery of lines
- **Full body appearance** — 3-5s, showing the full view
- **Wide / empty shots** — 4-6s, atmosphere rendering
- **Single shot no more than 6s** — Beyond 6s, audience attention declines; needs camera movement or dynamic elements to maintain
- **Golden 6-second rule** — If no new information (dialogue / action / subject change) appears for a cumulative 6s in a dialogue-free shot, audience attention breaks. Especially careful for establishing + transition shots — better to merge and compress than to drag

---

## V. Shot Merging Strategy (Removing AI Feel)

- **Don't split what one shot can cover** — If a single moving shot (e.g., slow push from wide to full shot) can simultaneously complete establishing + subject introduction, don't split it into "empty shot establishing first → then cut to subject entering the frame"
- **Merge consecutive similar information** — Consecutive shots describing different parts of the same space (gate → vines → scorched room) should be merged into one shot, with frame description covering multiple layers of spatial information
- **Narrative density priority** — Every shot must advance narrative or emotion. Purely decorative shots (only showcasing environmental details) should be merged into shots with narrative function
- **Director-thinking self-check** — After writing the storyboard, ask yourself: if a real-life director would merge 2-3 adjacent shots into 1, then the division is too fine — merge them

---

## VI. One-Shot Takes (Long Shot Merging)

- **Applicable conditions** — When adjacent shots have continuous action changes, mild scene changes (movement within the same scene), or gradual angle changes, prioritize using a one-shot take instead of fragmented cuts for smoother frame and content
- **Typical scenes** — Character walking through space, following an action from point A to point B, circling a character to show environment, establishing slow push to subject close-up
- **Annotation method** — In the camera movement field, write the complete path (e.g., "one-shot take: slow push wide → track into courtyard → land on full shot"), and in the frame description, describe the start frame and end frame
- **Duration relaxation** — Due to continuously updated information, can break through single-shot 6s ceiling, but no more than 12s
- **Generation risk** — One-shot takes have high requirements for frame generation continuity, making generation more difficult. Only use when the narrative fluidity benefit clearly outweighs fragmented cutting —no more than 2-3 instances in the entire film

---

## VII. Character Interaction Design

- **No more than two actions per single shot** — "Lowering head to pick a flower + smiling" works. "Lowering head to pick a flower + smiling + turning + raising a hand" will break
- **Sweet romance interactions use suggestion** — Fingers almost touching, sleeves brushing past, eyes following then looking away. Don't write "hugging" or "kissing" in the storyboard table for large-scale two-person interactions; break them into suggestive partial shots

---

## VIII. Dialogue & Silence

- **Shots with little dialogue need longer duration** — Silent emotional shots often need more time than those with dialogue. 3 seconds of silence has more tension than a line of dialogue
- **One line of dialogue corresponds to one shot** — Avoid stuffing multiple dialogue lines in a single shot. When the speaker changes, the shot should change
- **Narration shots use wide or empty shots** — Inner monologue paired with close-ups can look fake (lips not moving). Pairing with wide shots or scene empty shots is more natural

---

## IX. Transition Design

- **Default hard cuts** — Hard cuts between shots within the same scene, clean and crisp
- **Scene changes use empty shot transitions** — Insert 1 empty scene shot (2-3s) between different scenes as emotional buffer
- **Segment changes can use dissolves / fades** — Emotional jumps between major segments use soft transitions to prevent the audience from being pulled out of the story
