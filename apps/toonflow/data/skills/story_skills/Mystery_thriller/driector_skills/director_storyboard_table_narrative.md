# Storyboard Table Narrative Techniques · Mystery Thriller · Technique Reference

---

## I. Storyboard Table Positioning

The storyboard table is the director's core tool for transforming script into camera language. Form fields are set by the director according to project needs (shot number, shot scale, camera movement, duration, characters, events, dialogue, lighting, emotion, transitions, etc.). The following only provides technique references under the mystery thriller narrative type.

---

## II. Shot Scale Selection

- **Shot scale is information volume** — The core logic of shot scale in suspense storyboards is "how much information to let the audience see." For every shot, first ask: what should the audience know here? What should be hidden?
- **Selective close-ups** — Use close-ups to forcefully guide the audience's attention to a specific detail (a key, an expression, a line of text). But beware: overly obvious close-ups are equivalent to directly "drawing arrows" — the audience will guess it's a clue. The best clue close-ups have a "reason" (the character is looking at it/using it)
- **Obscuring medium shots** — The core value of medium shots in suspense is "showing enough but hiding the key." A medium shot of a character shows the action but obscures micro-expressions; a medium shot of a space shows the room but hides the corner
- **Revealing full shots** — Full shots are the most powerful reversal tool in suspense: cutting from a partial view to a full shot suddenly reveals "there's someone else in the room," "there's blood on the floor," "the window is open." The information explosion of a full shot is perfect for the reversal moment
- **Suspense implantation in establishing shots** — Establishing shots shouldn't just set the scene; implant a "wrong" detail in the establishing shot (a figure in a distant window, an extra chair in the yard). Complete establishing + suspense seed in at most 1-2 shots

## III. Camera Movement Rhythm

- **Default stable but not rigid** — 50% of shots are static camera positions, but key segments can introduce slight instability (subtle handheld, extremely slow panning) suggesting "something is wrong"
- **Slow push = discovery/approach** — Slowly pushing toward an object or character, suggesting "the audience is about to see something important." The slower the push, the stronger the suspense
- **Slow pull = revelation/full view** — Slowly pulling from a partial view to a full shot, revealing obscured information. This is the most classic "reversal camera movement" in suspense
- **Pan = scanning/searching** — Slow panning simulates "scanning a space," suitable for investigation scenes. The audience follows the camera to "search," enhancing engagement
- **Sudden stillness = discovery impact** — A moving camera suddenly stopping, creating a "freeze" effect. Used when the protagonist suddenly sees something they shouldn't
- **Peeking camera movement** — Slow push from behind an obstruction, with the foreground retaining the edge of the obstruction, simulating a third-person perspective of "someone watching from the shadows"

---

## IV. Duration Control

- **Lengthen suspense shots** — Key "discovery" shots in suspense can be 1-2s longer than normal, giving the audience time to "realize." A character staring at something for 3s is more unsettling than 1s
- **Normal duration for daily shots** — Keep shot duration normal (2-4s) in daily/illusion segments. Don't use abnormal duration to break the disguise
- **Stretching before reversal** — The last shot before the reversal shot should be appropriately lengthened (4-5s), creating the "calm before the storm"
- **Reversal moment: extremely short or extremely long** — The reversal reveal shot should be either extremely short (1-1.5s flash cut, impact) or extremely long (5-6s slow reveal, fear spreading), never in between
- **Golden 6-second rule still applies** — Unless there is camera movement or new information continuously input, single shots should not exceed 6s. Suspense's "slowness" is not about long shots, but the undulation of segment rhythm
- **Silent countdown** — Pure silent shots without dialogue or action are powerful tools in suspense, but should not exceed 4s, or the audience will think it's a technical glitch

## V. Clue Shot Design

- **Don't mark foreshadowing shots** — Do not use special markers in the storyboard table for clue-laying shots (avoiding "Note! This is a clue!" AI feel). Make clue shots look as natural as ordinary shots
- **Three principles for clue appearance** — ① Appears with a narrative reason (the character is using it/looking at it/passing by it); ② Not in the center of the frame (place at the edge or in the foreground); ③ No abnormal duration (pass through at normal rhythm)
- **Important clues appear at least twice** — First time: "passing by unintentionally"; second time: "meaning discovered." The interval between the two should be long enough that the audience ignores it the first time
- **Flashback shots need texture change** — Flashback shots in the storyboard must indicate visual differentiation from the present narrative (different color temperature, different shot scale tendency, different camera movement speed), allowing the audience to distinguish timelines at a glance
- **Prop continuity** — In suspense storyboards, changes in prop position are themselves narrative: the cup has been moved, the vase has been shifted, a photo is missing. Precisely describe the state of key props in the storyboard description

---

## VI. Information Revelation Strategy

- **Small reveals through dialogue, big reveals through visuals** — Secondary information can be conveyed through character dialogue, but core reversals must be accomplished through camera language (full shot reveal, close-up focus, flashback montage)
- **Reveal rhythm: first show the character, then the audience** — Classic rhythm for reversal shots: first film the character's expression change (shock/fear) → then cut to what the character sees (the audience learns the reason). The character's reaction "preheats" the audience's emotion
- **Or the reverse: first show the audience, then the character** — The audience sees the truth first (someone behind), while the character remains unaware — creating the anxiety of "turn around!" This technique requires full shots or over-the-shoulder shots to let the audience see information outside the character's line of sight
- **Give time to process after reversal** — Don't immediately advance the narrative after a major reversal. Leave 1-2 shots for the audience to digest (character stunned, empty shot, silence), letting the information volume settle

---

## VII. Character Behavior Design

- **Suspicious behavior needs a daily guise** — A character's suspicious behavior shouldn't be too obvious in the storyboard. "Repeatedly checking the watch" is better than "sneakily looking around" — the former could be innocent, the latter is too deliberate
- **Micro-expressions are the core clue carrier** — In suspense, a character's micro-expressions (slight pupil contraction, a fleeting change at the corner of the mouth, fingers lightly tapping the table) are more valuable than large-scale acting. Use near shots/close-ups to capture clue-related expressions in the storyboard
- **Ambiguous actions** — Designed character actions should allow multiple interpretations: a character wiping their hands could be drying them after washing, or wiping away some trace. Don't "explain" the meaning of the action through additional information in the storyboard; let the audience judge for themselves
- **No more than two actions per shot** — "Lowering head to read a letter + quickly folding it" works; "Lowering head to read a letter + folding + putting in pocket + looking around" will cause key actions to be drowned in information

---

## VIII. Dialogue and Silence

- **Unfinished sentences are the best suspense** — "Actually that night… forget it." A character trailing off is more unsettling than saying it. In the storyboard, pair this type of dialogue with a near shot + silent pause (2-3s)
- **One sentence per shot** — Avoid multiple lines of dialogue within a single shot, especially dialogue containing key information. Cut when the speaker changes
- **Ambiguous dialogue aided by expressions** — Lines with double meaning (normal on the surface, suspicious underneath) paired with the character's subtle expression. Use near shots in the storyboard to ensure the audience can capture the expression details
- **Restrained use of narration/inner monologue** — Suspense narratives prioritize visual storytelling. Narration is only used when the inner reasoning process must be conveyed. Pair narration shots with medium/long shots or empty shots to avoid the incongruity of a near shot with a mouth that doesn't move

---

## IX. Transition Design

- **Use hard cuts within the same scene** — Hard cuts between shots within the same scene keep things compact
- **Scene changes use black screen/flash white** — Use short black screens (0.5-1s) for scene changes in suspense, creating a sense of "interruption" that fits the suspense rhythm better than empty shot transitions
- **Flashbacks use match cuts** — Use similar composition/action for match cutting between present and recollection shots, reinforcing the causal connection (seeing a knife in the present → flashback to the scene where that knife appeared)
- **Time jumps use subtitle markers** — When the timeline is scrambled, transitions need clear time markers ("Three Days Ago," "At the Same Moment") to prevent the audience from getting lost
- **Use "suspense tail hooks" between segments** — Leave an unresolved suspense shot at the end of each segment before transitioning, maintaining forward momentum across segments
