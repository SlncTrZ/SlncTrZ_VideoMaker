# Narrative Technique · Mystery Thriller · Technique Reference

---

## I. Theme and Suspense Core

### Mystery Thriller Narrative Points

- **Information asymmetry is the essence of suspense** — The core of suspense narrative is manipulating the information gap between the audience and the characters. The director must be clear about what the audience "knows," "doesn't know," and "thinks they know" at every moment
- **Unreliable narration** — The camera can "lie": selectively displaying partial truth, using shot scales to obscure key information, using transitions to suggest false causal relationships. The audience trusts the camera — use this trust to create reversals
- **Layered truth design** — Don't reveal all the truth at once. Break the truth into 3-5 layers. Each layer's reveal should make the audience think "this time it's real," until the final reversal overturns everything
- **Suggested departure feeling** — Creepy / sudden realization / chills down the spine / lingering aftertaste. Avoid directions that don't match the suspense temperament, such as "lighthearted and happy" or "warm and healing"
- **Cracks in the everyday** — The best suspense doesn't start with the abnormal, but with "everything normal," then lets the audience gradually discover — something is wrong

## II. Narrative Structure and Suspense Rhythm

### Mystery Thriller Narrative Points

- **Hook first** — The opening must throw out a suspense hook within 30 seconds (an anomalous phenomenon, a sentence that shouldn't be spoken, an unreasonable image), making the audience ask "Why?"
- **Information release rhythm** — Follow the "give one, hide three" principle: for every piece of information released, create at least one new question. The amount of information increases, but so do the unresolved mysteries, until the climax segment where everything is settled collectively
- **Red herring strategy** — Deliberately design 1-2 misleading clues, allowing the audience to build a false reasoning. The misleading clues must be reasonable enough that when exposed, the audience feels "tricked, but convinced"
- **Use visuals, not dialogue, for turning points** — Key reversals should prioritize visual means (the camera suddenly revealing an obscured full view, contradictions between flashback images and present images, changes in prop position) over characters verbally explaining
- **Rhythm model: tighten → release → tighter** — Suspense rhythm is like winding a spring: gradually tighten → briefly release a false appearance → tighten more fiercely. Each release is the starting point for the next tightening
- **Timeline manipulation** — Mystery thrillers can scramble the timeline: start with a flashback (give the result first, then trace the cause), insert supplementary flashbacks (insert past clues at critical moments), parallel timelines (different perspectives on the same time). But every time jump must have a visual marker

## III. Scene Atmosphere Design

### Mystery Thriller Narrative Points

- **Describe atmosphere with concrete words** — Don't say "tense," say "the light at the end of the corridor suddenly went out one by one." Concrete atmosphere descriptions better guide set design and sound effect design
- **Typical suspense segments and design** —

| Segment Type | Emotional Direction | Narrative Technique | Music Suggestion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Daily/Illusion | Calm + Undercurrent | Everything normal but one "wrong" detail | Light daily background music with one dissonant note mixed in |
| Anomaly Discovered | Confusion + Curiosity | The protagonist notices something unreasonable | Music pulled away, leaving only environmental sound and a low-frequency hum |
| Investigation/Tracking | Focus + Unease | Gradually approaching the truth but the environment becomes more oppressive | Rhythmic background noise, gradually strengthening |
| Misdirection/Red Herring | Certainty + Relaxation | Seemingly finding the answer, the audience thinks it's over | Brief soothing melody (creating false security) |
| Reversal/Revelation | Shock + Creeping dread | A single shot overturns all previous understanding | Sudden silence, or a piercing single note |
| Truth Revealed | Relief + Aftermath | All clues retraced and connected, the truth puzzle completed | From oppression gradually into broad instrumental, but retaining uneasy aftertaste |

- **Spatial oppression is a narrative tool** — Use the degree of spatial closure to map the urgency of the suspense:
  - **Early**: open space, normal life, sufficient sense of security
  - **Mid**: space narrows (corridors, sealed rooms, alleyways), physical oppression rises
  - **Climax**: extremely enclosed (cornered beast) or extremely open (emptiness after truth revelation)
- **Visual clues must be "openly placed but subtly hidden"** — Clues should appear in the shot without being emphasized: placed at the edge of the frame, partially obscured by the foreground, glanced at by the character with no reaction. Ignored on first viewing, discovered on rewatching
- **Write the camera intention as "what to hide" rather than "what to show"** — "Using a near shot to prevent the audience from seeing the second cup on the table" is better than "Using a near shot to film the character's expression." The core of suspense storyboarding is designing "outside the frame"

## IV. Sound and Music Direction

### Mystery Thriller Narrative Points

- **Silence is more terrifying than a jump scare** — At key suspense moments (discovering an anomaly, about to reveal the truth), prioritize removing all sound, creating a vacuum-like sensation. People instinctively become tense in complete silence
- **Background noise creates unease** — Low-frequency hum, distant unidentified sounds, faint heartbeat — continuous background noise keeps the audience in a state of mild unease, even when the image looks normal
- **Sound effect misdirection** — Sound can "lie" just like visuals: let the audience hear footsteps outside the door and think it's the murderer, but it's actually an unrelated character. Sound misdirection is harder to detect than visual misdirection
- **Anomaly in environmental sound** — When marking core environmental sounds for each scene, consider adding a sound element that "shouldn't be there" (the distant sound of metal collision on a quiet night, an abnormal echo in a normal classroom), using sound to suggest "something is wrong"
- **Music emotion model** —

| Suspense Stage | Music Strategy | Coverage |
|---|---|---|
| Daily/Illusion | Normal background music hiding dissonant notes | Medium |
| Suspicion Emerges | Music fades, background noise rises | Low |
| Investigation Deepens | Rhythmic background noise, gradually oppressive | Medium-high |
| Pre-Reversal | Complete silence (vacuum feeling) | None |
| Reversal Moment | Single piercing sound / strong hit or sustained silence | Extreme |
| Truth Flashback | Broad instrumental with sad or uneasy ending note | Medium |

- **"Countdown" auditory cues** — Use gradually accelerating rhythmic elements (pendulum, dripping water, heartbeat speeding up) in segments approaching the truth to suggest time urgency, creating physiological tension more effectively than music

## V. Composition and Shot Scale Narrative

### Mystery Thriller Narrative Points

- **Narrative functions of the three core compositions** —
  - **Dutch angle (canted angle)** — World disorder/instability/character psychological distortion, used for key reversals or segments where the character's mental state is abnormal
  - **Obstructed composition** — Large foreground area blocking the subject, suggesting "peeping," "hiding," or "incomplete information"
  - **Deep focus composition** — Foreground and background both sharp, letting the audience choose where to focus — clues may be hidden in the overlooked depth of the scene
- **Limited use of center composition** — Center composition in suspense suggests "scrutiny/confrontation/no escape," reserved for high-pressure scenes like interrogations, confrontations, and truth revelations
- **Frame edges are clue zones** — In suspense composition, the edges and corners of the frame are the golden areas for hiding clues. The audience's attention is centered; place clues at the edges, only discovered on rewatching
- **Unease from symmetrical composition** — Overly perfect symmetrical composition in suspense creates a sense of "artificiality/unnaturalness," suggesting chaos hidden beneath surface order
- **Shot scale is information manipulation** — The core logic of shot scale selection is not "is it beautiful" but "how much does the audience see":
  - **Close-up** — Force the audience to see a specific detail (could be a clue, or a misdirection)
  - **Medium shot** — Provide enough information but obscure key parts
  - **Full shot** — Reveal the full picture, often used at reversal moments (turns out there's a third person in the room)
- **Function of extreme close-ups in suspense** — Not an emotional nuclear bomb but an "evidence magnifying glass": a blood-stained key, a face scratched out in a photo, a pair of slightly trembling hands. Every extreme close-up should convey a piece of information
- **Isolation in long shots** — Long shots in suspense emphasize the character's "smallness and helplessness": a lone figure walking across a vast snowy plain, a single lamp in an empty warehouse, a tiny silhouette in a maze-like city

## VI. Camera Movement and Rhythm

### Mystery Thriller Narrative Points

- **Peeking camera movement** — Slow push + foreground obstruction, simulating the perspective of "someone watching from the dark." The audience doesn't know "who" is watching, but can feel the unease of being watched
- **Uneasy handheld** — Slight handheld shake breaks stability, used for subjective segments after the character discovers an anomaly. The shake must be restrained — not documentary-style jittering, but the feeling of "hands involuntarily subtly trembling"
- **Sudden stillness** — A moving camera suddenly stopping, creating a "freeze" effect. Used at moments of major discovery (the character/audience suddenly noticing something wrong)
- **Fast cuts and suspense are not fully compatible** — Fast cutting suits chase/escape segments, but should be avoided in suspense construction segments. Suspense needs time to ferment;Choppy cutting interrupts thinking
- **360° orbit** — Suitable for interrogation/confrontation scenes, the camera slowly orbiting the character, creating the psychological oppression of being "surrounded/no escape"
- **Camera movement is information** — The core logic of camera movement is "guiding what the audience sees": slow push = guide attention to detail; slow pull = reveal the obscured full picture; pan = discover new information; sudden stop = information impact
- **Time manipulation camera movement** — Slow-motion for critical discovery moments, amplifying the character's micro-reactions; fast-motion for the anxiety of time passing. But slow-motion in suspense should be used extremely sparingly (1-2 times throughout the entire film)
