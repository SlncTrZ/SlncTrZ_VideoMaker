# Narrative Technique · Comedy & Humor · Technique Reference

---

## I. Thematic Conception & Comedy Core

### Comedy & Humor Narrative Key Points

- **Subverted expectation is the engine of laughter** — The underlying mechanism of every laugh is "subverted expectation": the audience expects the character to go left, they go right; expects them to cry, they burp. The director's job is to first establish expectation, then precisely break it
- **Character collision creates comedy** — The highest form of comedy doesn't rely on jokes, but on the chemistry of character personalities. Put a straight-laced person and a goofball together, and the dialogue is naturally funny. The greater the personality contrast, the stronger the collision
- **Absurdity must have internal logic** — When absurd things happen, the characters must "take them seriously." The more seriously the character takes it, the more absurd the audience finds it. If the character also finds it absurd, the joke disappears
- **Suggested exit feeling direction** — Belly laugh / Knowing smile / Laughter through tears / Absurd relief. Avoid directions like "fear," "heaviness," or "bleakness" that don't match the comedy temperament
- **Laughter through tears is the highest level** — Pure comedy makes people laugh and forget, but revealing a character's true vulnerability or kindness amid the absurdity, making the audience's eyes sting while laughing — this emotional contrast is comedy's most powerful weapon

## II. Narrative Structure & Joke Rhythm

### Comedy & Humor Narrative Key Points

- **Setup → punchline basic unit** — Every laugh consists of two parts: "setup" and "punchline." Setup builds expectation (the more serious, the better), punchline breaks expectation (the more unexpected, the better). Neither can be missing
- **Rule of three** — Classic comedy rhythm: first time establishes the pattern → second time reinforces the pattern → third time breaks the pattern. By the third time, the audience thinks it'll be the same — and it's not — that's the laugh
- **Escalating absurdity** — The level of absurdity should escalate step by step: small absurdity → medium absurdity → big absurdity. Starting with something too absurd makes it hard for the audience to accept. Gradually increasing the absurdity level maintains the comedy
- **Joke interval no more than 60 seconds** — In comedy narratives, the interval between two laughs should not exceed 60 seconds of narrative time. If there's no comedic output for over 60 seconds, the audience's "comedy expectation" cools off
- **Serious scenes are the best setup** — Serious/touching scenes interspersed in comedy don't interrupt the comedy — they serve as the longest setup for the next laugh. The more moved the audience, the greater the contrast with the following laugh
- **Misunderstanding chain drives the story** — The classic engine of comedy narratives is "misunderstanding": A misunderstands B → B misunderstands A's misunderstanding → C doesn't know A and B are both misunderstanding → the misunderstanding snowballs → finally the truth comes out, and all absurdity becomes laughable

## III. Scene-by-Scene Comedy Design

### Comedy & Humor Narrative Key Points

- **Describe comedy through contrast** — Don't say "funny," say "he performed CPR on a goldfish with a solemn expression." The concrete description of contrast itself makes people laugh
- **Typical comedy scenes and design** —

| Scene Type | Emotional Direction | Narrative Technique | Music Suggestion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Normal opening | Calm + Everyday | Everything normal but the character has a small quirk/obsession | Normal everyday score (building power for contrast) |
| Small loss of control | Accident + Amusing | A small accident triggers a chain reaction | Music continues normal (contrast amplifies comedy) |
| Misunderstanding escalation | Awkward + Absurd | The more they explain, the worse it gets; the more they try to fix, the more it collapses | Light rhythm accelerating to suggest loss of control |
| Major loss of control | Complete chaos | All characters simultaneously descend into their own absurdity | Cheerful music full blast or sudden complete silence |
| Genuine emotion | Laughter through tears | Real emotion suddenly revealed amid the absurdity | Music cuts abruptly, leaving only ambient sound |
| Return/final punchline | Relief + Knowing smile | One more small joke after everything settles | Easygoing closing melody |

- **Awkwardness escalation is the core comedy narrative** — The progression of awkwardness:
  - **Level 1**: Character embarrassed alone (saying the wrong thing, tripping)
  - **Level 2**: Others also witness the character's embarrassment (social death)
  - **Level 3**: Character tries to recover but makes it worse (the more they deny, the more they incriminate themselves)
  - **Level 4**: Complete loss of control, everyone is embarrassed (collective social death)
- **Space is comedy's accomplice** — Use spatial elements to create comedy: too many people crammed into too small a space, absurd things happening in a place that should be solemn, completely mismatched people in a completely mismatched location
- **Write "comedy source" not "what to shoot" for shot intent** — "Use a full shot so the audience can see there are ten more people standing behind him who don't know what's happening" is better than "Use a full shot to capture the scene." When the comedy source is clear, the storyboard naturally follows

## IV. Sound & Music Direction

### Comedy & Humor Narrative Key Points

- **Contrast scoring is a comedy weapon** — Absurd visuals paired with a dead-serious score (classical music/solemn march) are funnier than with cheerful music. The audio-visual contrast itself is a form of "subverted expectation"
- **Sudden silence = biggest laugh** — When lively music suddenly cuts out completely and the character does something utterly absurd on screen — the absurdity in silence hits harder than with music
- **Avoid "funny music"** — Don't use scores that "sound funny" (kazoos, comedic percussion sound effects). Let the visuals generate the comedy themselves; keep the music neutral or even serious to create comedy through contrast
- **Comedic function of ambient sound** — Specific ambient sounds can become part of the joke: a phone ringtone in the distance during a solemn speech, a sudden stomach growl in a quiet exam hall
- **Music mood model** —

| Mood Phase | Music Strategy | Coverage |
|---|---|---|
| Normal setup | Straight-faced score (contrasting with the upcoming absurdity) | Medium |
| Small loss of control | Music unchanged (pretending everything is normal) | Medium |
| Major loss of control | Music accelerates or suddenly stops completely | Extreme |
| Punchline release | Brief silence then resume | Low → Medium |
| Genuine emotion | Music cuts abruptly, pure ambient sound | None |
| Return to normal | Relaxed closing | Medium-low |

- **"Metronome-style" comedy** — Repetitive gags (the same mistake happening repeatedly) can use a fixed-rhythm background sound to reinforce the "here it comes again" expectation. The third time the rhythm breaks, the laugh is maximized

## V. Composition & Shot Size Narrative

### Comedy & Humor Narrative Key Points

- **Three core compositions and their narrative functions** —
  - **Reaction shot composition** — Place the joke-maker in the foreground/off-screen, and the reactor at the center of the frame. Often the bystander's expression is funnier than the joke itself
  - **Full-shot reveal composition** — The full shot reveals information the character doesn't know (someone standing behind them/their pants are torn/something absurd in the background), giving the audience one more layer of visibility than the character
  - **Off-kilter juxtaposition composition** — Two mismatched elements in the same frame: a man in a suit and a chicken, a solemn ceremony with a chaotic background. Off-kilter juxtaposition is itself comedy
- **The "spotlight" effect of center composition** — Center composition in comedy creates a "the whole world is watching you embarrass yourself" social-death feeling, reserved for the character's most awkward moments
- **Visual information gap** — The core of comedy composition is "the audience sees what the character doesn't": left side of the frame, the character is giving a serious speech; right side of the frame, someone is holding a banner with a typo
- **Shot size equals comedy rhythm** —
  - **Long shot/full shot** — Exposing the overall absurdity, revealing information the character doesn't know
  - **Medium shot** — Standard narrative, dialogue interaction, character collision
  - **Close-up** — Core shot size for reaction shots, capturing "Huh?!" and "What?!" expressions
  - **Extreme close-up** — Magnifying awkward details: twitching mouth corners, frozen smile, sweat on the forehead
- **Extreme close-up "freeze-frame punchline"** — Extreme close-ups in comedy function to "freeze and magnify": a misplaced hand, a misspelled nameplate, an expression that completely doesn't match. 1-2s is enough

## VI. Camera Movement & Rhythm

### Comedy & Humor Narrative Key Points

- **Calm observation is comedy's best perspective** — 60%+ of shots should be fixed camera, recording absurdity from the stance of a "calm bystander." The calmer the camera, the more starkly the on-screen absurdity stands out
- **Quick push = reaction amplification** — A character hears/sees something absurd, quickly push in on their face, amplifying the "frozen solid/shocked/unbelieving" reaction. This is the most frequently used camera movement in comedy
- **Whip pan = information reveal** — Quickly whip from character A to character B/something absurd. The speed itself carries a "wait, what's THAT?!" comedic quality
- **Stability vs chaos** — Use stable camera for setup scenes (everything normal); the camera can become slightly unstable or even handheld tracking for chaos scenes (following the chaos). The stable → unstable transition itself marks "here comes the funny part"
- **Camera movement equals rhythm** — Fixed = calm observation/setup; quick push = reaction amplification; whip pan = information reveal; handheld tracking = following chaos; slow push-in = discovering absurd details
- **Slow motion's comedic function** — Slow motion in comedy is used for the "moment disaster strikes": objects flying through the air, a person slipping, a cake hitting a face — slow motion gives the audience enough time to "see the disaster coming" without being able to stop it, the laugh building in anticipation
- **Don't move the camera too often** — Comedy doesn't need fancy camera work. The simpler and more plain the camera, the more the characters' absurdity stands out. Excessive camera movement distracts attention from the punchline
