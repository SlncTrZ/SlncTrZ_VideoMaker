# Storyboard Table Narrative Techniques · Comedy & Humor · Technique Reference

---

## I. Storyboard Table Positioning

The storyboard table is the director's core tool for translating scripts into camera language. Form fields are set by the director based on project needs (shot number, shot size, camera movement, duration, characters, events, dialogue, lighting, mood, transitions, etc.). The following provides technique references specific to the Comedy & Humor narrative genre.

---

## II. Shot Size Selection

- **Reaction shots are comedy's trump card** — Every punchline must be followed by a "reaction shot" (bystander's expression/character frozen in shock), captured in close-up. A punchline without a reaction shot is a dud
- **Full shots expose information** — The core function of full shots in comedy is to "let the audience see what the characters can't": the person behind them, the anomaly in the background, the impending disaster. Full shot = giving the audience a god's-eye view
- **Medium shots carry dialogue** — The primary shot size for character interaction/collision, maintaining enough information for the audience to see body language and expressions
- **Close-ups freeze awkwardness** — Close-ups in comedy magnify awkward details: a misspelled word, a misplaced object, a frozen smile. Brief (1-2s) but impactful enough
- **Establishing shots should plant a comedy seed** — The establishing shot in comedy can bury an "off" detail in an otherwise normal-looking scene (someone doing something strange in the background/a sign with a typo), setting up the punchline to come

---

## III. Camera Movement Rhythm

- **Fixed camera as default** — 60%+ of shots are fixed, the calm bystander perspective makes absurdity stand out more
- **Quick push = reaction amplification** — Quickly push in on the reactor's face after a punchline, amplifying shock/frozen solid/disbelief. The most frequently used camera movement in comedy storyboards
- **Whip pan = revealing absurdity** — Quickly whip from A to B, using speed itself to convey a "WHAT?!" sense of surprise
- **Tracking chaos** — When a character descends into chaos (chasing, fleeing, frantic scrambling), use handheld tracking; the shakiness conveys "everything is out of control"
- **Slow push-in = discovering details** — Slowly push in on an absurd detail, giving the audience a "wait, let me see that... HA!" discovery-style punchline
- **Camera movement shouldn't upstage** — The principle of comedy camera work is "don't upstage the characters." The simpler the camera movement, the better. Fancy camera work distracts attention from the punchline

---

## IV. Timing Control

- **Give setup shots enough time** — Setup shots 3-4s, allowing the audience to fully establish expectations. If the setup isn't thorough enough, the punchline won't land
- **Precise punchline beat** — Punchline delivery shots 1.5-2s, crisp and clean. The impact of a punchline lies in its "suddenness"; drag it out and it stops being funny
- **Reaction shots 2-3s** — Give the reactor/bystander enough "freeze frame" time for the audience to digest the joke
- **Comedic silence** — Leave 1-2s of "blank space" after a punchline (characters silent/air thick), giving the audience time to laugh. If you push the narrative forward immediately, the laughter gets cut off
- **Golden 6-second rule** — Comedy rhythm requires single shots to generally not exceed 4s (faster than other genres). Only setup and sincere emotional scenes can go to 5-6s
- **Rule of three timing progression** — When the same joke repeats three times: first time normal length → second time slightly shorter (audience already knows the expectation) → third time shortest or longest (breaking the pattern)

---

## V. Punchline Storyboard Design

- **Setup → punchline shot structure** — Setup shot (medium/close-up, character seriously doing something) → Punchline shot (full shot revealing absurdity/close-up magnifying the mistake/close-up reaction of frozen shock). Two shots form one joke unit
- **Visual gags don't need dialogue** — Purely visual jokes (character stepping on something, absurd event in the background, prop in wrong place) are presented in full shots or medium shots with camera movement, not reliant on dialogue
- **Information gap gags** — The audience knows something the characters don't, creating anticipatory comedy. In storyboarding: full shot gives audience information → cut back to character medium/close-up (character blissfully unaware) → disaster strikes
- **Chain reaction gags** — A small mistake triggers a domino-effect chain collapse. Storyboard uses fast hard cuts to link each "falling domino" (2s/shot), rhythm accelerating
- **Comedy scenes that can be done in one take shouldn't be cut up** — A long take where a character gradually goes from normal to chaos, the whole process in one take has more "irreversible" comedic effect than fragmented cuts

---

## VI. Multi-Character Comedy Blocking

- **Group scene reaction chain** — After a punchline, cut to each bystander's reaction in sequence: A stunned → B suppressing a laugh → C pretending not to see → D more embarrassed than the person involved. The reaction chain itself extends the comedy
- **Character collision uses shot-reverse-shot** — Two characters with contrasting personalities in dialogue use shot-reverse-shot, rhythm accelerating (1.5-2s per line). The collision is externalized through editing speed
- **Bystanders are the best comedy amplifier** — Always keep a "bystander position" in the storyboard: a passerby, an unrelated character, a pet — their reactions help the audience "confirm" that this thing is indeed absurd
- **Group chaos uses full shots** — When multiple people descend into chaos simultaneously, use a full shot in one take, letting the audience see all the absurdity at once. The information overload of the full shot is itself comedic

---

## VII. Dialogue & Rhythm

- **Dialogue beat determines the laugh** — The laugh in comedic dialogue lies in the beat: setup at normal speed → micro-pause before punchline (0.5s) → punchline delivered cleanly. The pause is the "fuse" of the laugh
- **Roasts must be fast** — Roast/comeback dialogue must land within 0.5s of the previous line's end. Quick counterattacks have a satisfying snap. In storyboarding, roast shots get no extra setup — cut directly to close-up
- **Silent comedy** — After an absurd event, the group goes silent for 2-3s — funnier than any dialogue. The micro-expressions during the silence (pretending nothing's wrong/frozen solid/slow head turn) are the comedy itself
- **The power of repetition** — Same line/same action repeated three times, the third time changes (says it wrong/does it wrong/even the speaker starts laughing). In storyboarding, all three times use the same shot size + same composition, third time breaks it
- **Voiceover as roast** — Voiceover in comedy can act as a "sarcastic commentator," describing absurd images in a calm tone. Voiceover paired with long shots or full shots works best

---

## VIII. Transition Design

- **Absurd contrast cut** — Hard cut from one extreme directly to another (character says "absolutely not" → next shot character is doing that very thing). The hard cut itself is the joke
- **Deadpan transition** — Use formal/solemn transition methods (fade in/out, title card "Three Hours Later") to bridge absurd content, the contrast creates comedy
- **Fast hard cuts within scenes** — Comedy scene editing rhythm tends to be fast, hard cuts primarily, no dawdling
- **Contrast montage** — Alternate between the character's spoken "beautiful plan" and the actual "disaster scene" execution. The ideal vs. reality contrast creates sustained comedy
- **Breaking the fourth wall** — When a character suddenly looks at the camera/speaks to the audience, use a direct close-up + sudden freeze, creating a conspiratorial "see what I mean?" feeling
