# Narrative Techniques · Horror & Supernatural · Technique Reference

---

## 1. Thematic Focus & Emotional Core

### Horror & Supernatural Narrative Key Points

- **The Unknown is Fear** — True horror comes from "what cannot be seen." Don't rush to reveal the full appearance of the monster/ghost. The audience's imagination is far more terrifying than any design. The thematic focus should revolve around "the unknown threat," not "the known monster"
- **Cracks in the Everyday** — The most effective horror comes from details of "wrongness" appearing in familiar daily life: a clock running backward, an extra chair, one more person in a photo. The focus should emphasize "the collapse of safety"
- **Slow Erosion** — Horror is not a one-time scare but the continuous accumulation of unease. The thematic design should have an erosion arc "from the subtle to the uncontrollable," letting the audience slide from "something doesn't feel right" to "completely unable to escape"
- **Suggested audience takeaway** — Lingering fear / chill down the spine / unsettling upon reflection / afraid to look back. Avoid purely sensory stimulation directions like "nausea" or "gore"
- **Humans are More Terrifying than Ghosts** — In the highest level of horror narrative, supernatural elements are merely metaphors for the dark side of human nature. The true source of fear should point to the human heart: greed, suspicion, betrayal, madness

## 2. Narrative Structure & Rhythm Planning

### Horror & Supernatural Narrative Key Points

- **Unease accumulation is the fundamental skill** — The core task of the first half of horror narrative is "accumulating unease." Use tiny anomalies in everyday scenes to gradually erode the audience's safety. Each segment's level of "wrongness" should be one degree higher than the previous one
- **Tension and release create psychological contrast** — Can't stay tense the whole time, or the audience will go numb. Insert "false safety" between horror segments (the character thinks the danger has passed), let the audience breathe — and then pull the tension taut again
- **Information control is the core engine** — The essence of horror is information asymmetry: the audience doesn't know what's there, the character doesn't know the danger is approaching. Narrative planning should clarify for each segment "what the audience knows" and "what the character knows"
- **Delayed reveal** — Key horror elements (the monster's true face, the truth, the source of the threat) should be revealed as late as possible. Reveal a little each time, creating a spiral of "the more you know, the more afraid you become"
- **Climax is the confirmation of fear** — All the accumulated unease from earlier is confirmed in the climax: "the worst premonition comes true." The climax is not the most frightening moment but the terrifying epiphany that "it's been... all along"
- **Blurring reality and illusion model** — An advanced horror narrative technique is making the audience unable to tell "whether it's real or the character's hallucination": reality → anomaly → doubt → confirmation → greater doubt. The blurrier the boundary between real and unreal, the stronger the unease

## 3. Scene-by-Scene Emotion Design

### Horror & Supernatural Narrative Key Points

- **Use embodied sensory words for emotional goals** — Don't say "scared," say "a sudden chill down the back" or "wanting to turn around but not daring to." Emotional descriptions at the embodied level better guide narrative rhythm and performance design
- **Typical emotional segments and design** —

| Segment Type | Emotional Direction | Narrative Technique | Sound Suggestion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Establishing everyday | Safety + warmth | Normal life scenes, deliberately creating a "everything is fine" atmosphere | Natural ambient sounds, relaxed atmosphere |
| Subtle anomaly | Vague unease | A detail that shouldn't be there, the character doesn't notice but the audience does | An incongruous sound mixed into the ambient noise |
| Safety collapse | Fear budding | Anomaly frequency increases, the character begins to sense "something is wrong" | Ambient sound gradually thins, low frequency enters |
| False safety | Brief relief | The crisis appears to be over, the character relaxes | Return to normal ambient sounds, music stops |
| Fear confirmed | Extreme terror | The worst suspicion is confirmed, no escape | Sudden silence, or extreme low-frequency oppression |
| Aftermath/lingering fear | Unsettling upon reflection | Seems to end but leaves one "wrong" detail | Normal ambient sounds with previous anomaly buried in |

- **"Deprivation of safety" is a core narrative tool** — Use the gradual erosion of a character's safe zones to map the escalation of fear:
  - **Early**: threat is far away, outside, in the dark
  - **Middle**: threat enters the living space, but escape is still possible
  - **Late**: threat is right beside/behind/inside, nowhere to run
- **Space as psychology** — Use spatial characteristics to convey fear. Enclosed space = nowhere to run; open space = nowhere to hide; familiar space distorted = collapse of safety
- **The "unspeakability" of fear** — The most terrifying thing should never be fully described. The character's reaction (dilated pupils, inability to speak, turning and running) is more effective than directly showing "that thing"

## 4. Sound & Music Direction

### Horror & Supernatural Narrative Key Points

- **Silence is the most powerful fear tool** — The killer weapon of horror films is not the scream sound effect but the absolute silence that precedes it. Key horror segments should design a "sound vacuum" — all ambient sound suddenly disappears, the audience holds their breath instinctively
- **Low-frequency pressure** — Low-frequency sounds (in the 18-20Hz range) that the human ear can't clearly hear but the body can perceive create physiological unease. Use low-frequency background noise in unease accumulation segments; the audience can't say why but they just feel uncomfortable
- **Unidentified sound sources** — The golden rule of horror sound effects is "you heard it but don't know what made it." Footsteps, breathing, scraping sounds — the more ordinary the sound, the more terrifying it is when it appears where it shouldn't
- **Ambient sound as fear thermometer** — Use changes in the layering of ambient sound to mark the level of fear: normal (birdsong/insect sounds/human voices) → thinning (sounds gradually disappearing) → dead silence → abnormal sounds appearing
- **Music emotional progression model** —

| Emotional Stage | Music Strategy | Coverage |
|---|---|---|
| Everyday safety | Normal scoring or pure ambient sound | Low |
| Unease budding | Dissonant intervals, very low volume | Medium-low |
| Fear escalation | Low-frequency noise gradually intensifying, rhythmic suggestion | Medium |
| Fear climax | Sudden silence or extreme sound effects | Extreme |
| False safety | Return to normal ambient sounds | Low |
| Lingering fear after | Normal sounds with abnormal elements mixed in | Medium-low |

- **Use "Jump Scare" sparingly** — Sudden loud shock scares (Jump Scare) should be used at most 1-2 times, and only after long periods of accumulated silence. Overusing jump scares is the cheapest horror technique and will dissipate the atmospheric fear accumulated beforehand

## 5. Composition & Shot Size Narrative

### Horror & Supernatural Narrative Key Points

- **Narrative functions of three core compositions** —
  - **Large areas of negative space** — Empty areas = potential threat zones. The audience will instinctively "search" the blank space for the source of fear
  - **Frame-obscuring composition** — Door cracks/windows/end of corridors. Restricted field of view = restricted information = magnified fear
  - **Unbalanced composition** — Subject biased to one side of the frame, leaving "unsafe space" on the other side,suggesting something is there
- **Restricted field of view is fear** — In horror narrative, the less the audience sees, the more afraid they are. Use shot size and composition to control information: don't let the audience see behind the character, don't let them see the corner of the room, don't let them see the full picture in the dark
- **Threat of spatial depth** — Use spatial depth to suggest "something in the distance": a blurry silhouette at the end of the corridor, something in the background that shouldn't have moved, an abnormal shape out of focus
- **Shot size and information control** — Close-up = the character doesn't know what's happening around them (the audience doesn't either); long shot = the character is exposed to the threat (the audience sees it but the character doesn't). Use shot size transitions to create different types of fear
- **Fear function of extreme close-ups** — Extreme close-ups (pupils/lips/fingers) in horror narrative capture the "physiological reactions of fear": dilated pupils, trembling lips, nails digging into palms
- **"Something extra"** — The core technique of horror composition: elements that shouldn't be in the frame appear (a second face in the mirror, a silhouette in the shadows, an extra figure in the background). The audience's fear upon noticing these far exceeds any sound effect

## 6. Camera Movement & Rhythm

### Horror & Supernatural Narrative Key Points

- **Stillness = oppression** — Over 70% of shots in horror narrative should be static. A still image prevents the audience from gaining the security of "someone is in control" through camera movement, creating the lonely fear of "being abandoned at the scene"
- **Extremely slow push-in = approach/inescapable** — A push-in even slower than normal,suggesting "something is approaching" or "you have to face it," suitable for fear confirmation segments
- **Slow pull-out = exposure/revealing the full picture** — Slowly pulling back to reveal the full horror the character hasn't seen (a figure behind them, abnormal room) — the classic technique of "the audience knows but the character doesn't"
- **Sudden stillness** — A moving camera suddenly freezes, suggesting "something has made the camera stop," creating extremely strong unease
- **Breathing camera movement** — Subtle slight shaking/fluctuation, simulating the subjective voyeuristic feeling of "someone secretly watching here." Not handheld shaking, but an extremely subtle "breath"
- **Camera movement as threat direction** — The direction of camera movement suggests the source of the threat. The audience instinctively feels that the direction the camera moves is the direction of "what we're about to see" — this expectation can be used, or it can be subverted to create a scare
