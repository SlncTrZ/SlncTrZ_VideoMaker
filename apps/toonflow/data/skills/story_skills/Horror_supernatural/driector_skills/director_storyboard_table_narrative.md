# Storyboard Table Narrative Techniques · Horror & Supernatural · Technique Reference

---

## 1. Storyboard Positioning

The storyboard is the director's core tool for translating the script into visual language. The form fields (shot number, shot size, camera movement, duration, characters, action, dialogue, lighting, emotion, transition, etc.) are set by the director according to project needs. The following only provides technique references for the horror & supernatural narrative genre.

---

## 2. Shot Size Selection

- **Shot size as information control** — The core logic of shot size selection in horror narrative is "controlling how much the audience knows." Close-ups = restricted character field of view (the audience doesn't know what's around either); long shots = character exposed to the unknown (the audience sees the danger but the character doesn't)
- **The enclosed fear of close-ups** — Close-ups/extreme close-ups turn everything outside the frame into "unknown territory," making the audience instinctively anxious about "what's outside the frame." Use more close-ups in fear accumulation segments to restrict information
- **The exposure fear of long shots** — Long shots make characters appear small, isolated, and exposed, suitable for segments where "the threat is nearby but the character doesn't know"
- **Avoid the safety of medium shots** — Medium shots are the "safest" shot size (moderate information, moderate distance). In horror narrative, reduce the use of medium shots and use the extreme switch between close-ups and long shots to create unease
- **Establishing shots must create unease** — Establishing shots should not just set the environment but embed "wrongness" from the first frame — a blurry shape at the edge of the frame, a window that shouldn't be lit, a space that is too quiet

---

## 3. Camera Movement Rhythm

- **Stillness by default, stillness as oppression** — Over 70% of shots should be static. In horror narrative, stillness is not "the absence of camera movement" but actively creating the suffocating feeling of "being trapped in this perspective"
- **Extremely slow push-in = approach** — Half the speed of a normal slow push-in,suggesting "something is slowly approaching" or "you have to see this thing"
- **Slow pull-out = revealing the full horror** — Slowly pulling back from a character or partial view to reveal a horrifying truth the character hasn't seen (someone behind them, the room has been destroyed, everything around is abnormal)
- **Sudden stop** — A moving camera suddenly freezes,suggesting "something has brought everything to a halt," creating an extremely powerful psychological impact
- **Micro-breathing tremor** — Extremely subtle frame fluctuations, simulating the subjective presence of a "voyeur." Not handheld shaking, but a subconscious perception that "someone is watching"

---

## 4. Timing Control

- **Lengthen fear shots** — The shot duration of unease/fear segments should be 50% longer than normal. Force the audience to "stay" on the unsettling image, waiting for a potentially horrific event
- **Give enough time to silent shots** — Silent shots (ambient sound vacuum) should last at least 3-4s, allowing the oppressive quality of silence to fully affect the audience
- **Scare moments are extremely short** — Jump scare shots should be kept to 1-1.5s, cutting away before the audience can react, leaving the afterwave of fear
- **Close-up lingering 3-4s** — Close-ups of fearful expressions need longer lingering than normal, allowing the audience to "catch" the character's fear
- **Long shots/empty shots 5-7s** — Long shots and empty shots for horror atmosphere need more time, giving the audience time to "search" the frame for abnormalities
- **Golden rule: fear comes from waiting** — The audience knows something terrible is going to happen but doesn't know when. Every second of waiting magnifies the fear

---

## 5. Shot Merging Strategy (De-AI-ification)

- **Continuous unease must not be broken** — Shots that continuously build an uneasy atmosphere should be merged into long takes as much as possible. Once broken (hard cut to a new angle), the accumulated unease is reset
- **Environment exploration in one take** — Character exploration of unknown spaces (entering a dark room, walking down a long corridor) should be done in one take as much as possible. Chopping it up destroys the immersive fear of "experiencing it with the character"
- **Narrative density first** — Every shot must advance the fear or narrative. Purely decorative atmosphere shots should be merged into shots with narrative function
- **Director's thinking check** — After writing the storyboard, self-check: Is the horror atmosphere continuous or interrupted by choppy cuts? If a director would use one long take to complete a segment, don't break it into fragments

---

## 6. One-Shot Sequences (Long Take Merging)

- **Applicable conditions** — Character exploring unknown spaces, chase/escape, gradual progression from safety to fear. The value of long takes in horror narrative is "not giving the audience a chance to breathe"
- **Typical scenes** — Character walking alone down a dark corridor, opening a door to explore a room, moving from a normal space into an abnormal space, chase and escape
- **How to annotate** — Write the complete path in the camera movement field (e.g., "One-shot: tracking character through corridor → slow push-in entering room → final frame: character's back stands still"), and describe the opening and closing frames in the visual description
- **Relaxed duration limits** — Horror long takes can exceed the 6s single-shot limit but should not exceed 15s, as they continuously create oppressive tension
- **Suffocation control** — The longer a one-shot sequence lasts, the stronger the audience's anxiety of "waiting for horror to happen." The long take itself is a fear generator

---

## 7. Character Interaction Design

- **Solo scenes are horror's home ground** — The most effective segments in horror narrative are often when characters are alone. The proportion of solo shots in the storyboard should be higher than group scenes
- **Fear reaction is more important than the source of fear** — What the character sees is not important; the character's reaction is what matters. The storyboard should prioritize shots of "the character's fear reaction" over shots of "the horror entity itself"
- **Restrained movement** — A person in fear does not move in large gestures but freezes/curls up/slowly retreats. In the storyboard, avoid writing "the character screams and runs away"; write "the character freezes, their fingers unconsciously clenching"

---

## 8. Dialogue and Silence

- **Speak less, fear more** — Dialogue in horror segments should be minimal. The more a character talks, the more "in control" they seem; silence allows fear to spread
- **Fragmentary dialogue** — Dialogue in fear should be fragmentary: half-sentences, names that can't be called out, a hushed "don't move." Complete sentences ruin the horror atmosphere
- **The audience knows what the character doesn't** — The best horror silence is when "the audience has already seen it but the character is still talking normally," using the character's unawareness to contrast the horror

---

## 9. Transition Design

- **Default hard cuts** — Use hard cuts between shots within the same scene — clean and sharp, maintaining tension
- **No buffer between horror segments** — Unlike other genres, transitions between segments in horror narrative should give the audience as little room to "rest" as possible, unless it's a deliberately arranged "false safety"
- **Black screen fear** — Horror narrative can use brief black screens (0.5-1s) as transitions. During the black screen, the audience can't see anything = maximum fear. But don't overuse it
- **Sound-first transition** — The frame is still in the previous scene, but the sound has already moved to the abnormal sound effects of the next scene. Use sound to "invade" the frame, creating a sense of fear permeation
