# Narrative Technique · Urban Workplace Drama · Technique Reference

---

## I. Thematic Conception & Workplace Core

### Urban Workplace Drama Narrative Points

- **Authenticity is the foundation** — The first priority of workplace narrative is "believability." The audience will verify every detail against their own experience; any loss of authenticity leads to disengagement. Scenes, dialogue, and character behavior must all withstand the scrutiny of real workplace people
- **Undercurrent of power gameplay** — Workplace conflict is not street fighting, but the struggle for speaking rights in meeting rooms, the politics of email CC, alliances and betrayals exchanged through a single glance. Pleasant on the surface, turbulent underneath
- **The cost of growth** — Workplace growth is not leveling up by defeating monsters. Every step upward comes with a cost: compromising principles, losing friends, enduring loneliness, self-doubt. The growth arc is not a straight line upward but a spiral
- **Suggested audience takeaway directions** — Resonance / reflection / feeling inspired / mixed emotions. Avoid directions that mismatch workplace realism like "thrilling," "stress-relieving," or "exhilarating"
- **The light of ordinary people** — The most moving workplace narrative is not "the big shot controls everything," but the faint light of ordinary people who still maintain kindness / professionalism / persistence amidst rules and pressure

## II. Narrative Structure & Gameplay Rhythm

### Urban Workplace Drama Narrative Points

- **Interest chain drives narrative** — The driving force of workplace stories is not "feelings" but "interests." Every character's behavior should have a clear interest motivation (promotion, keeping position, resources, face, security). The more specific the motivation, the more authentic the narrative
- **Information asymmetry creates tension** — "Who knows what" determines the power structure in the workplace. The narrative should skillfully manage information gaps between characters and between characters and the audience: the audience knows about the betrayal but the protagonist doesn't = anxiety; the audience and protagonist are both kept in the dark = empathy
- **Offense-defense transition** — The rhythm of workplace gameplay is offense-defense transition: protagonist gains upper hand → opponent counterattacks → protagonist on the defensive → finding a breakthrough → turning the tables. Each transition should have a clear "trigger event" (an email, a leak, a decision)
- **Use daily life as breathing between segments** — Insert daily life fragments (commuting, eating, being alone) between high-intensity gameplay segments, letting the audience and characters "catch their breath" while showcasing the authentic texture of workplace life
- **Stage-by-stage wins and losses** — Don't wait until the end for a result. Arrange a "small win" or "small loss" in each intermediate segment, letting the audience feel that progress and risk coexist
- **End with an aftertaste** — The ending of workplace narrative should not be too "perfect." Even if the protagonist wins, traces of the cost should remain (who was lost, what was compromised, relationships that can't return). Authenticity comes from "mixed emotions"

## III. Scene-by-Scene Tension Design

### Urban Workplace Drama Narrative Points

- **Describe tension with behavior** — Don't say "nervous," say "the pen cap was clicked unconsciously three times." Concrete behavioral details are more convincing than emotional labels
- **Typical workplace scene types and design** —

| Scene Type | Emotional Direction | Narrative Technique | Music Suggestion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Daily Work | Mundane + Real | Fragmented daily routine: commuting, meetings, typing | Very low volume urban white noise or no music |
| Undercurrent Gameplay | Calm on surface + Turbulence underneath | Eye contact warfare in meetings, voiceover of email exchanges | Low-frequency rhythmic background noise, crescendo |
| Conflict Eruption | Confrontation + Pressure | Direct confrontation, cards on the table, being exposed | Music suddenly stops or cold string music |
| Solitude / Breakdown | Fatigue + Real | Sitting alone in the car after work, washing face late at night | Pure environmental sound (water / distant cityscape) |
| Turnaround / Breakthrough | Calm + Determination | Discovering key information, making a decision, fighting back | Clean rhythmic music, gradual fade-in |
| Reconciliation / Cost | Relief + Complexity | Shaking hands but with distance in the eyes, loneliness after promotion | Warm but restrained instrumental |

- **Social distance as power expression** — Use the physical distance between characters to map power relationships:
  - **Superior**: Can freely approach subordinates (invading space); subordinates keep distance (respect / fear)
  - **Peer gameplay**: Sitting opposite / confronting, keeping a table's distance (gameplay distance)
  - **Close allies**: Sitting side by side / walking shoulder to shoulder (same direction = same camp)
- **Space is power** — Use office space to convey power relationships. For example: private office vs. open-plan cubicle = power hierarchy; seat position in meeting room = speaking rights; position in elevator = social hierarchy
- **Shot intent should write "power relationship" not "who to shoot"** — "Using a low-angle shot to make the audience feel the superior's pressure on the protagonist at this moment" is better than "using a low-angle shot to film the superior speaking"

## IV. Sound & Music Direction

### Urban Workplace Drama Narrative Points

- **Urban white noise is the atmospheric foundation** — Keyboard clatter, coffee machine, elevator hum, distant phone ringing — these sounds constitute the "smell" of the workplace, instantly immersing the audience in the scene
- **Silence is the highest form of gameplay** — Sudden silence in meetings, pauses after questions, not speaking during eye contact — silence is a weapon in the workplace. In key gameplay segments, prioritize removing music, leaving only environmental sound + silence
- **Extreme restraint in scoring** — Overall music coverage should not exceed 40%. Workplace narrative pursues "authenticity"; too much scoring makes it feel like an "inspirational ad" rather than an authentic story
- **Environmental sound to distinguish scenes** — Mark 1-2 core environmental sounds per scene to help distinguish spaces: office (keyboard + AC hum), meeting room (quiet + paper rustling), commuting (city noise), late night at home (fridge hum + distant traffic)
- **Music emotion model** —

| Emotional Stage | Music Strategy | Coverage |
|---|---|---|
| Daily Work | No music, pure environmental sound | None |
| Undercurrent Gameplay | Low-frequency rhythmic background noise | Low |
| Conflict Confrontation | Cold string music or complete silence | Medium |
| Solitude / Real | Pure environmental sound or very low piano | Extremely low |
| Turnaround / Breakthrough | Clean rhythmic music | Medium |
| Ending Aftertaste | Restrained warm instrumental | Low-Medium |

- **Narrative function of phone / message notification sounds** — Phone vibration, message notification sounds, phone ringing can be "fate-turning" triggers in workplace narrative — one message changes the entire situation. These sound effects should be given enough distinctiveness

## V. Composition & Shot Scale Narrative

### Urban Workplace Drama Narrative Points

- **Three core compositions and their narrative functions** —
  - **Power diagonal** — Place the power-holder in the upper / front part of the frame, the weaker person in the lower / back part; diagonal layout suggests unequal power relationships
  - **Framework trap** — Door frames / window frames / screen borders / cubicle partitions framing characters, conveying the oppressive feeling of "being trapped in the system"
  - **Symmetrical face-off** — Gameplay opponents sit on opposite sides of the frame; symmetrical composition suggests evenly matched confrontation (or surface equality hiding deadly intent)
- **Center composition's ritual sense** — Center composition is reserved for key "judgment" moments: promotion announcements, layoff notices, final decisions. Not used in daily life
- **Glass / reflections as barriers** — The workplace is full of glass (meeting rooms, office doors, elevators). Shooting through glass creates a sense of "visible but unreachable" barrier, suggesting the surface transparency but actual walls of workplace relationships
- **Vertical spatial oppression** — High-rise overlooking, elevator up-down, staircases ascending-descending — movement in vertical space directly maps "social hierarchy up and down"
- **Shot scale as social distance** —
  - **Wide / full shot** — Full view of space, the insignificance of people within a massive organization
  - **Medium shot** — Formal social distance, standard shot scale for meetings / conversations
  - **Medium close-up** — Breaking social distance, pressure / intimacy / confrontation
  - **Close-up** — Capturing subtle expressions: restrained smile at the corner of the mouth, evasive eyes, nervous fingers
- **Psychological function of extreme close-ups** — A pen being snapped, a message on a phone screen, the slightly trembling pen tip when signing. Extreme close-ups in the workplace amplify the "details of pressure"

## VI. Camera Movement & Rhythm

### Urban Workplace Drama Narrative Points

- **Calm fixed as default** — Over 65% of shots should be tripod-fixed camera positions, conveying a "calm observation" documentary quality. Workplace narrative doesn't need fancy camera movements; authenticity comes from restraint
- **Documentary handheld** — When following a character walking (hallways, commuting, hurrying), use slight handheld to increase the "being there" sense of reality. Shake is extremely restrained, almost imperceptible
- **Slow push = pressure / scrutiny** — Slow push toward a character's face, simulating the pressure of "being stared at." Suitable for segments of being interrogated, tested, or making a choice
- **Slow pull = isolation / full view** — Slow pull from character to full shot, revealing the character's smallness and loneliness in the vast office space. Suitable for segments of solitude, failure, late-night overtime
- **Horizontal pan = scanning / parallel** — Horizontal panning from one character to another, establishing spatial relationships. Suitable for scanning around a meeting room, group shots of open-plan office areas
- **Camera movement as narrative attitude** — Static = calm observation; slow push = approaching / pressure; slow pull = withdrawal / reflection; handheld tracking = authentic participation; horizontal pan = scanning the whole picture
- **Quick-cut montage** — In workplace narrative, quick cuts are only used for "time-passing montage" (day-after-day work fragments, all-night project fragments), not for everyday narrative
