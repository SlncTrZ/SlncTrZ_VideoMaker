# Storyboard Table Narrative Technique · Urban Workplace Drama · Technique Reference

---

## I. Storyboard Table Positioning

The storyboard table is the director's core tool for converting script into camera language. Table fields are defined by the director according to project needs (shot number, shot scale, camera movement, duration, characters, events, dialogue, lighting, emotion, transitions, etc.). The following provides technique references specific to the urban workplace drama narrative type.

---

## II. Shot Scale Selection

- **Shot scale is social distance** — The core logic of shot scale in workplace storyboarding is "the sense of distance between people." Formal occasions use medium shots (social distance); only go to medium close-up for confrontation / intimacy
- **Medium shot is the primary shot scale** — Medium shots are used most frequently in workplace narrative (40-50%), because the workplace itself is a social field of "keeping appropriate distance"
- **Medium close-up breaks distance** — Medium close-up in the workplace suggests "distance has been broken": it may be trust (private heart-to-heart) or pressure (superior approaching). Every use of medium close-up means a change in relationship
- **Close-up captures subtext** — In the workplace, everyone wears a mask; true emotions are hidden in subtle expressions. The core function of close-ups is to "expose the mask": tension under a restrained smile, disdain during a nodding agreement, hesitation when signing
- **Establishing shots convey power space** — Workplace establishing shots are not just "this is an office"; they should convey the power suggestion in the space: who sits in the main seat, who is near the door, whether the space is open or enclosed. 1-2 shots to get it done

---

## III. Camera Movement Rhythm

- **Fixed camera position as default** — Over 65% of shots fixed, conveying a calm and objective observation perspective
- **Documentary handheld** — When following a character walking / commuting, use extremely subtle handheld, increasing authenticity but with almost imperceptible shake
- **Slow push = pressure / scrutiny** — Slow push toward a character's face, suitable for segments of being interrogated, making a decision, or bearing pressure
- **Slow pull = isolation / overall view** — Slow pull from character to full shot, revealing the individual's smallness within a massive system
- **Horizontal pan = scanning group shots** — Horizontal panning across multiple characters or workstations, establishing group relationships and the full spatial picture
- **Camera movement is attitude** — Fixed = calm observation; slow push = pressure / focus; slow pull = withdrawal / isolation; handheld = authentic participation

---

## IV. Duration Control

- **Dialogue shots at standard rhythm** — Shots with dialogue: 2-4s, one cut per line, maintaining dialogue rhythm and information clarity
- **Silent gameplay extended** — Silence shots in confrontation / gameplay: 3-4s, letting silence itself become a weapon. The audience can feel "the words that weren't said"
- **Daily fragments at fast pace** — Commuting, typing, drinking coffee — each daily fragment 1.5-2s is enough, fast fragmentation conveys daily rhythm
- **Solitude shots get duration** — Sitting alone in the car, washing face late at night, standing by the window — these "real moments" get 4-5s, letting the audience feel the character's fatigue after taking off the mask
- **Golden 6-second rule** — Single shot no more than 6s. The power of workplace narrative lies in information density, not duration. Better to add another angle than to drag out one shot
- **Montage fragments** — Time-passing fragments: each fragment 1-1.5s, quickly stacking to convey the oppressive feeling of "day after day"

## V. Dialogue Storyboard Design

- **Power position cross-cutting** — In dialogue storyboarding, who is in the upper / lower part of the frame suggests power relationships. Low-angle shot of superior + high-angle shot of subordinate is the classic power cross-cut
- **Over-the-shoulder shot's power suggestion** — In an over-the-shoulder shot, "whose shoulder" appears in the foreground = who currently has the speaking right. The suppressed character uses the other's shoulder as foreground obstruction
- **Triangular relationship composition** — Three-person gameplay scenes use triangular composition, with the three people occupying three positions in the frame. As positions shift, the triangular composition dynamically adjusts
- **Subtext using expression shots** — After a line with hidden meaning, cut to a 1-2s listener reaction shot (medium close-up / close-up), letting the audience read the subtext from the listener's micro expression
- **Meeting full shot → medium close-up progression** — Meeting segments start from full shot (overall view), and as conflict escalates, gradually progress to medium close-up / close-up (focusing on the confronting parties). Shot scale progression maps tension progression
- **Don't split what one shot can cover** — If a single panning shot can simultaneously show the speaker and the listener's reaction, don't split it into two reverse-angle shots

---

## VI. Solitude Segments

- **The real after work** — Leaning against the wall with eyes closed in the elevator, driving home in silence, collapsing on the sofa — these segments are the most precious "real moments" in workplace narrative. Use medium-wide shots + fixed camera position, give enough duration
- **Restrained breakdown** — A workplace person's breakdown is not wailing, but deep breathing in the restroom, reddened eyes, hand on forehead. Use medium close-up to capture restrained breakdown, which resonates more than exaggerated performance
- **Late-night reflection** — Late-night solitude reflection / review uses wide shots + environmental empty shots, with city lights outside the window suggesting "countless equally tired people in this city"
- **Narrative function of commuting segments** — Commuting is not a transition but narrative itself: tired faces on the subway, helplessness in traffic jams, deep breath before entering the office — these moments of "switching personas" are the most authentic rituals of workplace people

---

## VII. Dialogue & Subtext

- **Words with hidden meaning are the norm** — Workplace dialogue is rarely literal. "This proposal could still use some optimization" = rejection; "I respect your decision" = disagree but don't want to argue. The storyboard should use expression shots to help the audience interpret the subtext
- **Silent offense and defense** — The silence after asking a question, the silence waiting for a response after making a proposal — silence in workplace dialogue is a strategy of "forcing the other side to speak first." Give silence 2-3s as an independent shot
- **One line of dialogue, one shot** — Key gameplay dialogue occupies a single shot, not shared with other lines. Pair this line with a medium close-up + listener reaction
- **Narration / inner monologue is extremely rare** — Workplace narrative relies on the contrast of "words don't match actions" (saying "I'm fine" while face says "there's a problem"), not on narration to explain inner thoughts. Narration is only used when information must be supplemented
- **Focus management in group dialogue** — In multi-person meetings, not everyone needs a cut when speaking. Use full / medium shots to show the overall view, only cut to medium close-up to focus on key speakers

---

## VIII. Transition Design

- **Hard cuts within the same scene** — Hard cuts between shots within the same meeting room / office,maintain clean rhythm
- **Daily flow using fragment montage** — Day-after-day workplace routine uses fast fragment montage (alarm → commute → clock in → meeting → overtime → go home), each fragment 1-1.5s
- **Spatial contrast transition** — Hard cut from busy office to quiet home at night, using spatial contrast to convey the split feeling of "two worlds"
- **Time jumps using subtitles or empty shots** — When the time span is large, use subtitles ("Three months later") or seasonal / weather change empty shots to suggest
- **Use commuting as inter-segment transition** — Commuting segments are the natural paragraph dividers of workplace narrative: commuting to work = new segment begins; commuting home = segment closure
- **Phone screen transition** — Message notifications / emails / news push notifications as transition triggers: focus on phone screen → new information → introduce new segment
