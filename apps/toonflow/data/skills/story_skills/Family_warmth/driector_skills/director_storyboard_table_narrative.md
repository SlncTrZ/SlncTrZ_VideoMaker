# Storyboard Table Narrative Techniques · Family Warmth · Technique Reference

---

## 1. Storyboard Positioning

The storyboard is the director's core tool for translating the script into visual language. The form fields (shot number, shot size, camera movement, duration, characters, action, dialogue, lighting, emotion, transition, etc.) are set by the director according to project needs. The following only provides technique references for the family warmth narrative genre.

---

## 2. Shot Size Selection

- **Medium shots are the backbone of family narrative** — Most family narrative scenes involve "family members in one space." Medium/wide shots ensure multiple people are in frame, conveying the sense of belonging that comes from "being together"
- **Close-ups capture love that goes unspoken** — Much family emotion is not expressed through words. Close-ups capture: trembling hands, secretly reddened eyes, a suppressed smile, an extra serving of food silently added to someone's bowl
- **Long shots for watching over** — Long shots in family narrative are primarily used for "seeing off/waiting": the figure at the doorway seeing someone off, the silhouette by the window waiting. Give long shots enough duration (5-6s) to fully convey the emotion of "watching over"
- **Extreme close-ups for emotional overflow moments** — Extreme close-ups (teary eyes/clenched fists/quivering lips) are reserved for moments when emotion can no longer be contained. 2-3 times per episode is sufficient
- **Establishing shots use life details** — Family narrative establishing shots don't use empty architectural exteriors; use life details: a steaming kitchen, neatly arranged slippers by the shoe cabinet, notes stuck to the refrigerator

---

## 3. Camera Movement Rhythm

- **Stillness by default, stability means warmth** — Over 70% of shots should be static. In family narrative, stillness conveys a reassuring sense of "steadiness/constancy/reliability"
- **Slow push-in = noticing a warm detail** — Slowly push in on a warm detail (a meal left on the table, shoes placed at the door, a blanket re-tucked) to let the audience "discover" the unspoken love
- **Slow pull-out = leaving/seeing off** — Slowly pull back from a character, suitable for farewells, leaving home, gradually exiting a warm family scene
- **Slow tracking shot = everyday companionship** — Follow a character's daily routine at home, using the steadiness of camera movement to convey "the rhythm of daily life in this home"
- **Observer's perspective** — The camera is like a quiet family member, silently watching from the sidelines. Not participating, not judging — simply present with tenderness

---

## 4. Timing Control

- **Everyday shots 3-4s** — Everyday shots (eating/chatting/doing chores) maintain a relaxed but not dragging pace
- **Silent shots 4-5s** — Silent companionship between family members (sitting together without talking, each doing their own thing) needs enough duration for the audience to feel that "silence itself is happiness"
- **Detail close-ups 2-3s** — Close-ups of warm life details (a cup of hot tea/a neatly folded piece of clothing) need enough lingering time for the audience to "read" the love behind them
- **Long shot farewells 5-6s** — Long shots of farewells/waiting need more time for the "watching over" emotion to fully develop
- **Conversation close-ups 3-4s** — Family conversation has a relaxed rhythm, with natural silent pauses between each sentence
- **Golden rule: unhurried** — All pacing in family narrative should have "the ease of home." Haste = anxiety, ease = reassurance

---

## 5. Shot Merging Strategy (De-AI-ification)

- **Single-shot everyday flow** — Family everyday segments (cooking → serving → sitting at the table → eating) should be covered in one long take with camera movement whenever possible. Chopping them up destroys the "continuity of everyday life"
- **Dining table scene in one take** — A family meal scene that can be covered with one slow panning shot across everyone should not be broken into individual fragmented shots
- **Narrative density first** — Every shot must advance the narrative or convey emotional temperature. Purely decorative spatial displays should be merged into shots that serve a narrative function
- **Director's thinking check** — After writing the storyboard, self-check: Do everyday segments have a smooth sense of life? Do emotional segments have enough lingering time? Do quick cuts break the steady rhythm of home?

---

## 6. One-Shot Sequences (Long Take Merging)

- **Applicable conditions** — Family daily routines, coming home from the doorway to the dining table, the complete process of cooking to serving. The value of long takes in family narrative is "letting the audience stay in this home for a while"
- **Typical scenes** — Character enters → changes shoes → walks to the kitchen → sees the meal left on the table; the whole cooking process (washing vegetables → cutting → cooking → plating); seeing a child to the door → watching them walk away
- **How to annotate** — Write the complete path in the camera movement field (e.g., "One-shot: character pushes door open → tracking shot through the living room → arrives at kitchen to see the meal on the table → final frame: character stops and smiles"), and describe the opening and closing frames in the visual description
- **Relaxed duration limits** — Family everyday long takes can exceed the 6s single-shot limit but should not exceed 12s, as information is continuously updated and a sense of life rhythm is needed
- **Life rhythm first** — The movement speed of a long take should match the character's life rhythm — neither too fast nor too slow, conveying "the daily life of this home"

---

## 7. Character Interaction Design

- **Family interactions use habitual actions** — Interactions in a family are not improvised but "habitual": always serving each other extra rice, always sitting in the same spot, always calling each other's name the same way
- **Show unspoken love through action** — In storyboards, family emotional transmission should prioritize action shots: adding an extra serving of food, pushing a favorite dish toward someone, the first thing upon entering the door is checking if the other person is there
- **Physical distance equals sense of security** — Intimacy between family members is "approaching without needing a reason": naturally leaning on a shoulder, unconsciously holding hands, a gentle pat when passing by

---

## 8. Dialogue and Silence

- **Everyday words carry the most weight** — The most touching dialogue in family narrative is everyday speech: "Have you eaten?" "Go to bed early" "Take it slow on the road" "Call me when you get there." No need for profound lines — daily concern is the deepest love
- **Important words are the hardest to say** — Key emotional expressions in family narrative should prioritize silence/action over words. "I love you" can't be said, but one can silently add an extra bowl of soup for the other person
- **One sentence per shot** — Family conversation has a slower rhythm, with natural silence between each sentence. Don't cram too much dialogue into one shot

---

## 9. Transition Design

- **Use temporal rhythm for everyday transitions** — To express the repetitiveness of family daily life, use temporal rhythm transitions: "morning → noon → evening" or "spring → summer → autumn → winter"
- **Use life details for scene transitions** — Use a life detail shot (a steaming pot/clothes drying on the balcony/a lit window) as a bridge between different scenes
- **Use slow dissolves for segment transitions** — Between major segments (e.g., from "everyday life" to "farewell"), use slow dissolves to convey the gentle passage of time
- **Default hard cuts** — Use hard cuts between shots within the same scene to keep the narrative clean and natural
