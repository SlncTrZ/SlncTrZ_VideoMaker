# Narrative Technique · Coming of Age · Technique Reference

---

## I. Thematic Conception & Emotional Core

### Coming of Age Narrative Key Points

- **The preciousness of firsts** — The core appeal of coming-of-age narratives lies in the impact of "firsts": first crush, first failure, first goodbye, first time standing up for someone else. Thematic conception should revolve around the vividness and fluster of "experiencing a certain emotion for the first time in life"
- **Clumsiness is authenticity** — The way youth expresses itself is inherently clumsy. Wrong words spoken, wrong things done, the courage to miss and desperately chase after — perfect youth isn't touching, flustered youth is
- **Time's irreversibility is the underlying emotion** — All the sweetness and regret in coming-of-age narratives share a common undertone: "we can never go back." This irreversibility gives weight to even the smallest moments
- **Suggested exit feeling direction** — Nostalgia / Relief / Smile with tears / Want to go back to that day. Avoid utilitarian directions like "satisfaction" or "comeback"
- **The pain of growth** — Coming-of-age narratives can't just be sweet. True growth comes from pain: cracks in friendships, shattered ideals, first realization that "the world isn't what I thought." Sweetness is the surface, but pain is the engine of growth

## II. Narrative Structure & Rhythm Planning

### Coming of Age Narrative Key Points

- **Fragmented storytelling is the natural form** — Youth memories are inherently fragmented: a sentence during break, the sunset on the way home, sudden rain on some day. The narrative structure can embrace more fragmented scene organization, connected by emotional lines rather than event lines
- **Emotional rollercoaster** — Emotional changes in youth are intense: laughing until your stomach hurts one second, eyes reddening the next. Emotional transitions between scenes can be more abrupt than in other genres — this itself is the texture of youth
- **Growth arc is not a straight line** — A character's growth is not "doesn't understand → understands" in a straight line, but the winding path of "thinks they understand → gets proven wrong → truly understands." Every time they think they've grown up, they find they still have far to go
- **Dual timeline of memory and present** — Coming-of-age narratives often use the dual-timeline structure of "now looking back at the past." The memory timeline is full of vivid details, while the present timeline gives these details the weight of "never going back"
- **Ensemble convergence and separation** — The core narrative model of youth ensembles is "gathering → experiencing together → going separate ways." What's most tear-jerking isn't the separation itself, but "we thought we'd be together forever"
- **The "That Day" model** — Every important turning point in coming-of-age narratives should be tied to a specific "that day": the day it rained heavily, the day of the sports festival, the day school let out especially early. Specific time anchors give memories texture

## III. Scene-by-Scene Mood Design

### Coming of Age Narrative Key Points

- **Use youthful somatic words for emotional goals** — Don't say "happy," say "laughing so hard they bent over then suddenly made eye contact and froze." Don't say "sad," say "only dared to cry after walking out the school gate." The emotions of youth are always mixed and contradictory
- **Typical emotional scenes and design** —

| Scene Type | Emotional Direction | Narrative Technique | Music Suggestion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Everyday playfulness | Energy + Carefree | Group play, fragmented daily life, fast-paced editing | Light rhythm, guitar/whistle/hand claps |
| First crush budding | Heart flutter + Nervousness | Stealing glances, deliberately taking the same path, electric touch of fingers | Gentle melody, rhythm suggesting heartbeat |
| Friendship test | Hurt + Stubbornness | Misunderstanding, cold war, eating lunch alone, pretending nothing's wrong | Reduce music, ambient sounds highlight isolation |
| Struggle/competition | Tension + Passion | Countdown pressure, team coordination, last push | Drumbeat building, accelerating rhythm |
| Farewell/graduation | Reluctance + Relief | Emptying the drawer, last walk together, yearbook signing | From lively to quiet, ending with just one sound |
| Looking back/growth | Nostalgia + Warmth | Returning to old places years later, opening old keepsakes, feels like yesterday | Warm instrumental slow entry, with a touch of wistfulness |

- **"Spatial memory binding" is a core narrative tool** — In coming-of-age narratives, specific spaces should be bound to specific emotions, recurring to form emotional resonance:
  - **Classroom/playground**: Everyday/collective memory/sense of belonging
  - **Way home**: Secret crush/alone time/private thoughts space
  - **Secret base** (rooftop/under a tree/old warehouse): Friendship/promises/space that belongs only to us
- **Seasons equal mood** — Use seasonal changes to convey time passage and emotional shifts: Spring = budding/first meeting; Summer = passion/conflict; Autumn = parting/maturity; Winter = reflection/looking back
- **The "person beside them" perspective** — An advanced technique in coming-of-age narratives is "seeing the protagonist's changes through the people around them" — the silhouette a teacher sees through the window, the expression changes a friend secretly notices, the parent realizing their child no longer needs to be picked up

## IV. Sound & Music Direction

### Coming of Age Narrative Key Points

- **Youth has its own soundscape** — School bell, sneakers squeaking on the floor, pages turning, distant whistle from the field, click of a bicycle chain. These sounds form the "sound texture of youth." Each scene should mark 1-2 iconic youth sound effects
- **Music as a memory anchor** — Theme music in coming-of-age narratives has a special function: when the same melody plays in different scenes, the audience automatically recalls previous emotional memories. Suggest using different arrangements of the same theme for key scenes
- **Silent growth** — The moments when a character truly "grows up" are often silent: no longer arguing, no longer chasing, standing still to watch them leave. These growth nodes should remove background music, leaving only ambient sound
- **Ambient sound equals era** — Use ambient sound to convey the era: school broadcast exercise music, pop songs from street-side shops, summer cicada sounds. Sound evokes era resonance more than images
- **Music mood progression model** —

| Mood Phase | Music Strategy | Coverage |
|---|---|---|
| Everyday playfulness | Light rhythm, energetic instrumentation | Medium-high |
| Hidden feelings | Gentle melody, low volume | Medium-low |
| Conflict eruption | Music cut abruptly or urgent rhythm | Extreme |
| Reconciliation warming | Theme melody warm variation | Medium |
| Farewell exit | From ensemble to single instrument gradually | Medium → Low |

- **The distance between laughter and tears** — The most touching sound design in coming-of-age narratives is "laughter suddenly turning into sobbing" or "crying while laughing." The rapid emotional switch doesn't need musical accompaniment — natural human voice is enough

## V. Composition & Shot Size Narrative

### Coming of Age Narrative Key Points

- **Three core compositions and their narrative functions** —
  - **Ensemble composition** — Multiple people in frame/side by side/sitting in a circle, conveying a sense of belonging and collective memory of "we are together"
  - **Foreground-background composition** — One person in front, one behind, conveying the feeling of "watching you without you knowing" — secret crush/gazing
  - **Symmetrical composition** — Two people walking side by side/sitting on the same bench, conveying "equal companionship"
- **Shot sizes change with relationships** — When characters first meet, shot sizes are more distant and scattered (each in different parts of the frame); as the relationship warms, shot sizes tighten (same frame/close-ups); during separation, shot sizes widen again (matching long shots of each)
- **Spatial emotional memory** — When the same space appears repeatedly in different scenes, composition should subtly change to convey emotional shifts: the same road home, from two people side by side to one person walking alone; the same classroom seat, from full to empty
- **Dual function of long shots** — In coming-of-age narratives, long shots can convey both "freedom/vastness" (running on the field, gazing from the rooftop) and "smallness/loneliness" (a person walking alone on an empty street after school)
- **Youth function of extreme close-ups** — Extreme close-ups capture fleeting micro-expressions in youth: the corner of a mouth holding back a smile, reddened earlobes, eyelashes blinking away tears
- **Perspective anchoring of "That Day"** — Composition in memory scenes can deliberately adopt a "perspective from memory": imperfect angles, partial obstruction, as if secretly glimpsed from somewhere

## VI. Camera Movement & Rhythm

### Coming of Age Narrative Key Points

- **Alternating motion and stillness is the youthful rhythm** — Camera rhythm in coming-of-age narratives should frequently switch between "energetic movement" and "still gazing." Running → suddenly stopping → looking back — this rhythm itself is youth
- **Tracking running = signature coming-of-age camera movement** — Following a character as they run (chasing a bus/chasing someone/racing/sprinting) is the most recognizable camera movement in coming-of-age narratives. Keep the tracking slightly unsteady to convey the vitality of "running forward with all you've got"
- **Slow push-in = discovery/noticing** — Psychological cue for "noticed that person." Suitable for realizing a crush, discovering a friend is crying, noticing a change
- **Slow pull-out = leaving/growing up** — Psychological cue for "leaving this place/person behind." Suitable for farewells, graduation, realizing everything has changed
- **Still gazing = growth moment** — When a character truly grows up, the camera should stop and quietly watch. No push, no pull, no pan — just watch this person change in this moment
- **Camera movement equals emotional temperature** — Shaky tracking = energy/chaos; smooth slow pan = warmth/everyday; still = contemplation/growth; slow pull-back = farewell/relief
