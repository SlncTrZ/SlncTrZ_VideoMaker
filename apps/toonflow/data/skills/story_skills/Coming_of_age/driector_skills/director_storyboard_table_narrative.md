# Storyboard Table Narrative Techniques · Coming of Age · Technique Reference

---

## I. Storyboard Table Positioning

The storyboard table is the director's core tool for translating scripts into camera language. Form fields are set by the director based on project needs (shot number, shot size, camera movement, duration, characters, events, dialogue, lighting, mood, transitions, etc.). The following provides technique references specific to the Coming of Age narrative genre.

---

## II. Shot Size Selection

- **Prioritize ensemble shots** — Much of coming-of-age storytelling consists of group scenes (classroom/break time/parties/games). Full shots and medium shots are the workhorses of group scenes, ensuring multiple people share the frame to convey a sense of "being together"
- **Close-ups capture micro-expressions** — Much emotion in coming-of-age stories is conveyed through micro-expressions (suppressed smiles, red-rimmed eyes, nervous swallows). Place close-ups at key emotional nodes to capture these fleeting moments
- **Long shots for freedom and loneliness** — Long shots serve a dual function in coming-of-age narratives: a long shot of running on the field = freedom/energy; a long shot of an empty street after school = loneliness/contemplation. Choose based on the emotional goal
- **Extreme close-ups should feel youthful** — Extreme close-ups (reddened earlobes/clenched fists/eyelashes blinking away tears) capture moments where "the mouth says nothing but the body is honest"
- **Establishing shots use slice-of-life imagery** — Establishing shots in coming-of-age narratives don't need grand wide angles. A desk with sticky notes, a chair back with a jersey hanging on it, a corner piled with exam papers — that's enough

---

## III. Camera Movement Rhythm

- **Alternating stillness and motion** — 50% static camera + 30% tracking/slow push-ins + 20% handheld micro-shakes. The camera movement rhythm in coming-of-age narratives should itself have a quality of "bouncing around one moment, quieting down the next"
- **Tracking running shots** — Following characters as they run is a signature camera movement in coming-of-age narratives. Keep it slightly unsteady to convey vitality. Suitable for chasing someone, hurrying, race sprints
- **Slow push-in = noticing/discovering** — Psychological cue that the audience (and character) is noticing someone/something. Suitable for realizing a crush, noticing a friend acting strange, seeing a graduation notice board
- **Slow pull-out = leaving/letting go** — Slowly pulling away from a character/scene. Suitable for farewells, graduation, walking away alone
- **Handheld micro-shake = everyday realism** — Use slight handheld shake in everyday group scenes to add a sense of "being there" and the documentary quality of "recording as it happens"

---

## IV. Timing Control

- **Everyday fragments 2-3s** — The fragmented shots of lively everyday life are fast-paced, each shot 2-3s. Use quantity and rhythm to convey "every day flies by so fast"
- **Extend emotional scenes 4-5s** — Shots in emotional scenes (first love, arguments, reconciliation) should be 50% longer than everyday shots, giving emotions time to develop
- **Silent shots 3-4s** — Wordless coming-of-age epiphany shots (a character standing still watching others walk away) need enough duration for the audience to feel "in this moment, they grew up"
- **Long shots/empty shots 4-6s** — Seasonal empty shots, campus empty shots mark the passage of time. Give them enough duration for the audience to "feel time passing"
- **Dialogue close-ups 3-4s** — Coming-of-age dialogue rhythm tends to be faster, but key dialogues (confessions/arguments/apologies) should be appropriately slowed down
- **Golden Rule: Fragments build depth** — The emotional depth of coming-of-age narratives doesn't come from a single long take, but from the accumulation of many fragments. Better to have more 2-3s fragments than one dragging long take

---

## V. Shot Merging Strategy (De-AI-fication)

- **Keep everyday fragments as quick cuts** — Unlike other genres, in coming-of-age narratives the quick cuts of lively everyday scenes (rapidly switching everyday snippets) are themselves a narrative technique. No need to force-merge them
- **Complete emotional scenes in one take** — Key emotional scenes (confessions/arguments/reconciliations) should be done with complete long takes rather than quick cuts whenever possible, giving emotions space to develop continuously
- **Group scenes in one take** — For multi-person group scenes, if one shot with camera movement can cover it, don't break it into fragmented individual cuts. Maintain the holistic sense of "we are together"
- **Director's mindset check** — After writing the storyboard, self-check: Do everyday sections feel fragmented? (yes = correct). Do emotional sections feel continuous? (yes = correct). Is there contrast in rhythm between the two? (yes = correct)

---

## VI. One-Shot Takes (Long Take Merging)

- **Applicable conditions** — Character traversing campus, chasing/running, emotional transitions from one space to another. The value of long takes in coming-of-age narratives is "following this person through this moment"
- **Typical scenes** — Character running through hallways to the field, chasing after a bus, walking through an empty classroom after graduation, searching for someone in a crowd
- **Marking method** — Write the complete path in the camera movement field (e.g., "One-shot take: Track character rushing out of classroom → crossing hallway → pushing open door to the field → end frame character stops and looks back"). Describe the start and end frames in the visual description
- **Relaxed duration** — Due to continuous following and information updates, may exceed the 6s single-shot limit, but not more than 12s
- **Emotional continuity** — Emotions within a long take should have an arc of change (from anxious to relieved/from happy to disappointed). Don't let the long take become an emotional plateau

---

## VII. Character Interaction Design

- **Group scene interactions should have layers** — In multi-person playful scenes, each character should have their own small actions/expressions. Not everyone doing the same reaction. Layering = realism
- **Youth interaction is clumsy** — Pulling hands back after touching, wanting to speak but stopping, deliberately taking another path then stealing a glance — the core of youthful interaction is "wanting to get close but not knowing how"
- **Physical distance equals relationship** — Mark distance changes between characters in the storyboard: strangers sit one seat apart, friends sit shoulder to shoulder, after a fight each stands on one side, after making up they lean together again

---

## VIII. Dialogue & Silence

- **Youth dialogue should be short and fragmented** — Conversations in youth are fragmented: half-finished sentences interrupted, mid-sentence retractions, saying "it's nothing" while the face tells everything
- **Important words left unsaid** — The most powerful dialogue in coming-of-age narratives is often the words "that couldn't be said." Use silence, turning away, pretending nothing's wrong to replace direct expression
- **Overlapping voices in group scenes** — In coming-of-age group scenes, multiple people talk at once, interrupt each other, chattering away — don't aim for every word to be heard. The chaos itself is the sound of youth

---

## IX. Transition Design

- **Everyday scenes use fast-cut montage** — To express everyday scenes where "every day is similar but every day is precious," use fast hard-cut fragment montage: class → break → after school → class → break...
- **Emotional turning points use empty-shot transitions** — When emotions undergo major changes, insert a seasonal/campus empty shot for emotional buffer
- **Time spans use contrast transitions** — Same scene, same angle, but details have changed (different content on the blackboard/different people in the seats/leaf colors have changed). Use hard-cut contrast to convey the passage of time
- **Default hard cuts** — Use hard cuts between shots within the same scene to maintain the brisk feel of youth
