# Storyboard Table Narrative Techniques · Hot-Blooded Youth · Technique Reference

---

## I. Storyboard Table Positioning

The storyboard table is the director's core tool for transforming script into camera language. Form fields are set by the director according to project needs (shot number, shot scale, camera movement, duration, characters, events, dialogue, lighting, emotion, transitions, etc.). The following only provides technique references under the hot-blooded youth narrative type.

---

## II. Shot Scale Selection

- **Shot scale is power expression** — The core logic of shot scale in hot-blooded storyboards is "conveying power and emotional intensity." Use high-angle long shots for low points (sense of insignificance), and low-angle close-ups for eruptions (sense of power)
- **Low-angle is the core weapon** — Low-angle shots inherently carry psychological suggestion of "tallness/immovability." Must use low-angle shots when the protagonist stands up, declares their belief, and delivers the final blow
- **High-angle creates desperation** — High-angle shots make the subject appear small and helpless. Use high-angle shots when defeated and fallen, or facing an overwhelming enemy, creating maximum contrast with the subsequent low-angle shot
- **Extreme close-ups are for critical moments** — Extreme close-ups (pupils/fists/blood drops) are emotional nuclear bombs, used only 1-2 times per entire segment. Place them at the last moment before eruption to detonate emotions
- **Establishing shots must have oppressiveness** — Establishing shots in hot-blooded narratives don't just set the scene; they must convey oppression through space: a massive arena, endless ruins, the towering silhouette of the enemy. 1-2 shots suffice, long shots are enough

---

## III. Camera Movement Rhythm

- **Buildup segments are primarily static** — 70% of shots in the pre-eruption buildup segments are static camera positions, allowing silence and expressions to accumulate emotion
- **Eruption segments release kinetic energy** — Switch to moving shots like quick pushes, tracking, and pans during attack/awakening moments to release accumulated kinetic energy
- **Quick push = strike impact** — Abruptly push toward the subject, simulating the release of power. Used for the first frame of an attack, the moment of awakening with open eyes
- **Orbit = hero freeze-frame** — Slow orbit around the protagonist, used at most 1-2 times throughout the entire film, for the most thrilling "hero moment"
- **Slow-motion camera movement = time expansion** — Use slow-motion for the key strike, amplifying the power and detail of the impact moment. No more than 2 slow-motion shots per entire segment
- **Camera movement is the direction of power** — Push = advance/attack; Pull = buildup/retreat; Pan = scanning the battlefield; Track = walking side by side; Orbit = hero's gaze

---

## IV. Duration Control

- **Lengthen buildup shots** — Shots like "standing up," "clenching fist," "opening eyes" before eruption should be given 4-5s, or even 6s with slow-motion, to fully accumulate emotion
- **Shorten impact shots** — Compress the moment of attack/collision/hit to 1-1.5s (flash cut). Speed is impact
- **Slow-motion golden moments** — Slow-motion shots can exceed the 6s upper limit, but not more than 8s. Information is conveyed through continuous detail output (flying debris, flowing hair strands, vibrating ground)
- **Normal rhythm for daily shots** — Keep warm/everyday segments at a normal 2-4s rhythm. Don't use combat rhythm; let the audience relax
- **Golden 6-second rule** — Non-slow-motion shots should not exceed 6s. The "power" of hot-blooded narratives doesn't rely on dragging out duration, but on rhythm contrast
- **Contrast rhythm formula** — Last shot before eruption (slow/long) → first shot of eruption (fast/short). Extreme duration contrast creates maximum impact

---

## V. Combat Shot Design

- **Alternating offense-defense rhythm** — Combat segment storyboards cannot be all offense: attack → defend → retreat → attack again. Give and take creates tension. Pure one-sided domination has no narrative quality
- **Power comparison using shot scale** — When the enemy is strong and we are weak: use low-angle full shots for the enemy (sense of enormity) and high-angle medium shots for the protagonist (sense of smallness). Reverse when the tide turns
- **One strike decides victory** — Give the final blow of the ultimate duel full ceremonial feeling: buildup (slow-motion close-up) → strike (flash cut quick push) → hit (extreme close-up + impact silence) → result (slow pull to full shot). Complete four-beat presentation
- **Wounds are growth** — The protagonist's wounds must have close-ups in the storyboard: blood drops, cracks, trembling hands. Pain is the visualization of "cost." The greater the cost, the more weight the victory carries
- **If one shot can convey it, don't split into two** — A single long take with camera movement (e.g., tracking the protagonist from being knocked back to charging again) has more impact than fragmented shots. Combat long takes can exceed 6s

---

## VI. Comrade Narrative

- **Side-by-side composition** — When comrades are in the same frame, use horizontal composition, with both on the same horizontal line, implying equality and trust
- **Trust-transmitting shots** — An exchange of glances, a wordless nod, a gesture of turning one's back — trust doesn't need dialogue. Use near-shot cross-cutting to accomplish it
- **Duration of sacrifice shots** — Give sufficient duration (4-5s) to the shot of a comrade blocking ahead/falling, paired with the protagonist's expression close-up reaction. Don't rush through it; this is a tear-jerking climax
- **Ceremonial feel of return shots** — When a comrade arrives at a critical moment: first show the protagonist's desperate expression → sound/light change from behind → slow pull revealing the comrade's figure. Complete in three beats, not all at once

---

## VII. Dialogue and Shouts

- **One belief declaration is enough** — The protagonist's belief declaration (e.g., "The people I want to protect are right behind me!") should be carefully selected for 2-3 lines throughout the entire film. Don't repeat naggingly. One line with a low-angle near shot maximizes power
- **Silence is more powerful than declaration** — Use silence + action to replace dialogue in awakening/rising segments. The audience can read belief from the action; the character doesn't need to say it
- **Restraint in battle cries** — Pure "Ah——!" style battle cries should be used at most 2 times throughout the film, placed at the ultimate eruption moment. Paired with a quick push + extreme close-up
- **Opponent's dialogue** — The opponent's taunts/doubts are tools for buildup: "You can't win," "Give up" — these lines are fuel before the eruption. Use near shots to film the opponent speaking, then cut to a silent close-up of the protagonist
- **Very little narration** — Hot-blooded narratives rely on action. Narration is only used when inner monologue must be supplemented, paired with long shots or empty shots

---

## VIII. Transition Design

- **Use hard cuts within the same battle** — Hard cuts between shots within a battle keep the rhythm compact
- **Shockwave transitions** — Use flash white (0.3-0.5s) for transitions during ultimate move/eruption moments, simulating the visual effect of a shockwave
- **Black screen for buildup** — A short black screen (0.5-1s) can serve as a "deep breath" before eruption, with the next frame being the first frame of the eruption
- **Empty shots for breathing room between segments** — Use 1-2 environmental empty shots (sky, setting sun, falling petals) between combat and daily life segments as emotional transitions, giving the audience room to breathe
- **Flash cuts for recollection flashbacks** — Belief images flashing through the protagonist's mind before eruption (comrades' smiles, promises to protect), use 0.5-1s flash cuts for quick insertion without interrupting the rhythm
