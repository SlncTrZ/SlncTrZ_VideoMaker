# Storyboard Table Ink-Wash Constraints · Ink and Watercolor Spatter · Technique Reference

---

## I. Storyboard Table Positioning

The storyboard table is the director's core tool for translating scripts into visual language. The following provides only the constraint specifications for the Ink and Watercolor Spatter style at the storyboard table level.

---

## II. Color and Atmosphere

- **Wash scheme binding** — Each storyboard table row must specify the wash scheme (A-F) that governs the scene. Shots within the same scene must maintain the same primary wash scheme; scheme shifts are only permitted at scene boundaries or narrative turning points, marked clearly in the transition notes
- **Ink coverage floor** — Every shot must contain at least 40% ink elements (C1 Ink Black lines, washes, or spatter). A shot without ink presence violates the style anchor. Ink may recede only in extreme negative-space shots, but never below 25%
- **Bloom color lock** — Only one dominant bloom color per scene. If Vermillion Accent (Scheme B) is selected, no competing bloom from Jade Green or Plum Purple may appear. Secondary colors appear only in diluted washes (lightness +20% minimum)
- **Warm/cool wash discipline** — Warm and cool wash zones within a single frame must not intermingle chaotically. If the scene uses a warm bloom (Vermillion/Ochre/Blush), cool areas are restricted to ink shadows only. If using a cool bloom (Indigo/Jade/Plum), warm areas exist only as tiny light-source accents
- **Paper ground preservation** — Every shot must leave at least 15% negative space (pure C2 Warm White or near-paper tone). This is non-negotiable. Full-coverage shots require a justification note in the storyboard table

---

## III. Wash Dynamics

- **Wash evolution between shots** — When transitioning between shots within the same scene, the wash may intensify (more pigment, darker tones) or dilute (more water, lighter tones) to follow the emotional arc. Wash intensity must change monotonically within a scene — either building or receding, not oscillating
- **Spatter as emotional punctuation** — Spatter technique entries in the storyboard table must specify: density (light / moderate / heavy), color (must match scene bloom), and trigger (the narrative beat that causes the spatter). Spatter without clear emotional cause is prohibited
- **Bleeding edges at scene seams** — Adjacent scenes with different wash schemes should use bleeding transitions at the boundary (one wash bleeding into another). The bleed zone occupies exactly 2-3 frames and the dominant color gradually shifts from Scheme A to Scheme B

---

## IV. Action Pacing in Ink Style

- **Deliberate, brush-like movement** — Character actions default to the pace of a brush moving across paper — deliberate, irreversible, carrying the weight of each moment. Quick, fluttering movements are reserved for emotional climaxes, equivalent to spatter energy. Camera cuts should match the rhythm of brush strokes — one action per shot, no rushed sequences
- **Wash-responding motion** — When a character moves through a scene, the ink wash around them should respond — darkening where emotion gathers, lightening where they find resolution. Motion and wash dynamics are synchronized; a character walking from sorrow to acceptance should literally walk from Indigo shadow into Warm White light as the wash shifts
- **Silence preserves paper** — Periods of inaction or stillness are not empty — they are negative space given time. Hold on stillness for 2-3 seconds minimum before cutting. These moments allow the viewer to see the paper texture, feel the grain, and breathe with the composition

---

## V. Camera Movement Taboos

- **Steadicam / gliding camera prohibited** — Smooth continuous movement contradicts the brush-stroke nature of this style. Each frame should feel like a deliberate painting. Camera movement, if any, must be slow push-in or pull-out only, at a pace matching brush application
- **Handheld / shaky camera prohibited** — The ink-wash world is contemplative and grounded. Unstable camera language breaks the meditative quality of the paper surface
- **Dutch angles / canted frames prohibited** — Tilted horizons and disoriented angles have no equivalent in ink-wash painting. The frame must remain level, with emotional tension created through wash density and spatter, not camera geometry

---

## VI. Transitions Taboos

- **Hard cuts prohibited between scenes of different wash schemes** — Always use an ink wash dissolve: one scene's wash dissolves into the next through a brief moment of abstract wet-ink blending (2-3 frames). The dissolve color should be the mixing of both schemes' dominant colors
- **Wipe / slide / zoom transitions prohibited** — Digital transition effects are incompatible with the wet-media feel. The only approved transition is the ink wash dissolve described above
- **Flash cuts prohibited** — Rapid alternation between shots violates the contemplative ink-wash rhythm. Every cut must be motivated by a brush-stroke logic — the completion of one visual idea before the next begins
