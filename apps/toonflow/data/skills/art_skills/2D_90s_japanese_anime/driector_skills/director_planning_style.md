# Japanese Animation Constraints · 90s Japanese Anime · Technique Reference

---

## I. Color System and Visual Tone

- **Color base** — The entire film uses warm yellow (C1), cherry blossom pink (C3), and sky blue (C6) as base colors, with an overall warm color temperature (4800-5200K), medium-low saturation (50-70%), presenting the warm nostalgic feel of 90s hand-drawn animation. Maintain a soft warm tone base throughout the film, with localized cool colors (light blue C6, light purple C7) adding depth
- **Warm/cool narrative contrast** — Cool colors (light blue C6, light purple C7) serve as visual signals for narrative shifts, used as localized accents during nighttime or emotional turning points. Warm/cool switching should synchronize with the story arc, not be mixed arbitrarily
- **Color palette first principle** — Segment planning must first bind the emotional scene (everyday life / heartthrob / farewell / reunion, etc.), then determine the primary + secondary colors and lighting scheme, avoiding "right plot but wrong emotional color"
- **Prohibited color range** — High-saturation fluorescent colors, neon colors, and modern CG rendering color systems are all incompatible with this style

---

## II. Lighting Scheme System

- **Lighting is narrative** — 6 lighting schemes correspond to different emotional segments. At the director's planning stage, the lighting tone direction should be determined at the segment level, not shot by shot
- **Light source angle** — Default 30-45° side lighting, simulating natural light effects

| Emotional Segment | Lighting Direction | Tone Tendency | Applicable Phase |
|---|---|---|---|
| Everyday Warmth | A · Soft diffused | Warm yellow base + even soft light | Entire film |
| Heartthrob Moment | B · Warm side light | Cherry blossom pink + warm orange accents | Escalation phase |
| Office / School | C · Top light + ambient light | Neutral warm + soft cool white | Work / study phase |
| Romantic Sunset | D · Backlit warm tones | Amber warm dominant + cool blue background | Sweet phase |
| Night Moonlight | E · Cool moonlight | Light blue dominant + warm color accents | Night phase |
| Memory / Flashback | F · Soft-focus warm light | Warm yellow dominant + misty effect | Memory phase |

- **Warm/cool light distribution** — Warm tones can be used throughout the entire film, flexibly adjusted according to scene needs; cool tones (light blue dominant) are only used after nightfall or emotional shifts
- **Atmosphere direction mapping** — Each scene's atmosphere direction should map to one of the above lighting schemes (A-F), ensuring visual consistency

## III. Texture Direction

- **Flat coloring realism** — The core of 90s hand-drawn animation: clear flowing lines, even coloring, distinct lighting layers
- **Clear line art + delicate coloring** — This is the anchor point of visual texture. Not blurry smudging, not modern CG rendering
- **Flat coloring does not mean simplistic** — This style emphasizes the textural expression of 90s hand-drawn animation (clear line art, flat coloring, lighting layers), amplifying impact through composition and emotional design, rather than relying on complex effects
- **Hand-drawn does not mean rough** — 90s Japanese anime emphasizes the visual expression of hand-drawn textures (natural light positioning, soft colors, delicate expressions), amplifying impact through camera and emotional design, rather than relying on spectacular effects

## IV. Japanese Scene Space Elements

Characteristic scene elements of 90s Japanese anime and their visual narrative functions:

- **Window frames / door frames / corridors** — Natural framing composition tools, creating layered depth and spatial dimension that "cannot be seen through"
- **Classroom / rooftop / street** — Natural carriers of youthful atmosphere; scene equals emotion: sunlight by the classroom window = youth, long shadows on sunset streets = nostalgia, warm-lit home dining table = family warmth
- **Cherry blossoms / falling leaves / rain curtain** — Natural carriers of negative space composition; scene equals emotion: cherry blossoms in full bloom = heartthrob, falling leaves swirling = parting sorrow, walking alone in rain = loneliness
- **Sunset / moonlight / streetlamps** — Light source carriers of Japanese animation; sunset = warmth / romance (Scheme D), moonlight = cold / solitude (Scheme E), streetlamp = nighttime everyday life
- **Use scene-only shots for transitions between segments** — This style has rich scene assets (different times of day / weather / weather variants). For segment transitions, use scene-only shots as emotional buffers, avoid hard cuts
- **Use visuals rather than dialogue for turning points** — Prioritize visual means (lighting changes, shot size jumps, empty shot metaphors) over relying on dialogue for explanation

## V. Japanese Animation Instruments and Environmental Sounds

Sound element constraints within the 90s Japanese anime worldview:

### Instrument Selection

- **Piano** — Core instrument for everyday / warmth / memory segments, best expresses the texture of 90s animation
- **Guitar** — Lighthearted / romantic / street segments, the gentle strumming suits everyday atmosphere
- **String ensemble as foundation** — Emotional elevation / farewell segments, the delicate bowing suits emotional transitions
- **Music box / wind chimes** — Memory / dream segments, the crisp tones suit nostalgic atmosphere

### Instrument Combination Strategy

| Emotional Phase | Instrument Combination |
|---|---|
| Calm / Opening / Closing | Music box solo or wind chimes solo |
| Everyday Warmth | Piano + light guitar |
| Emotional Elevation / Romance | Strings + piano |
| Memory / Dream | Music box + piano |

### Japanese Environmental Sounds

- **Typical environmental sound layers** — Cicadas buzzing / insects chirping / train track sounds / wind through leaves / raindrops on eaves / street ambience / birdsong / wind sounds
- **Mark 1-2 core environmental sounds per scene** to assist subsequent sound design. The richer the environmental sound layers, the more immersive the Japanese scene
- **Silence is more important than music** — For key emotional moments (eye contact, turning away, farewell), prioritize removing music and keeping only environmental sounds (wind, cicadas, train sounds), letting the audience feel the emotion themselves
- **Avoid over-scoring** — The full film's music coverage rate should not exceed 50%. The "silence" of quiet segments and music segments create breathing room, highlighting genuine emotion
